Description

Atmosphere models the vertical pressure, density, and temperature structure of a planetary atmosphere in hydrostatic equilibrium. Starting from a measured sea-level (or reference-level) pressure and a user-supplied temperature profile, the program integrates upward in altitude until the pressure becomes negligible, then plots whichever of the three atmospheric quantities the user selects.

The program is a direct Python port of Schutz's Java Atmosphere program described in Investigations 7.1 and 7.3 of Gravity from the Ground Up (Cambridge University Press, 2003). It is suitable for modeling Earth's atmosphere, comparing planetary atmospheres across the Solar System, and exploring how scale height depends on gravity and molecular weight.

Textbook reference: Bernard Schutz, Gravity from the Ground Up, Cambridge University Press (2003), Chapter 7 — Investigations 7.1 and 7.3.

Physics & Equations

Hydrostatic Equilibrium

A column of atmosphere in static equilibrium satisfies the condition that the pressure gradient exactly balances the weight per unit volume of the gas:

\[ \frac{dp}{dh} = -g\,\rho \tag{7.1} \]

where \(p\) is pressure (Pa), \(h\) is altitude (m), \(g\) is the local gravitational acceleration (m s−2), and \(\rho\) is the mass density (kg m−3). The negative sign reflects the fact that pressure decreases with increasing altitude.

Ideal Gas Law

The density of an ideal gas is related to its pressure and temperature by

\[ \rho = \frac{p\,\mu\,m_p}{k_B\,T} \tag{7.2} \]

where \(\mu\) is the mean molecular weight (dimensionless, e.g. 28.97 for dry air), \(m_p = 1.67\times10^{-27}\;\text{kg}\) is the proton mass, \(k_B = 1.38\times10^{-23}\;\text{J K}^{-1}\) is the Boltzmann constant, and \(T\) is the temperature in Kelvin.

Substituting Eq. (7.2) into Eq. (7.1) gives the complete governing equation for atmospheric structure:

\[ \frac{dp}{dh} = -\frac{g\,\mu\,m_p}{k_B\,T(h)}\,p \tag{7.3} \]

For an isothermal atmosphere this integrates to the barometric formula \(p = p_0\,e^{-h/H}\), where \(H\) is the scale height.

Scale Height

The scale height \(H\) sets the characteristic altitude over which the pressure drops by a factor of \(e\). At the base of the atmosphere it is

\[ H = \frac{p_0}{g\,\rho_0} = \frac{k_B\,T_0}{g\,\mu\,m_p} \tag{7.4} \]

where \(\rho_0\) is the density computed from Eq. (7.2) at the surface pressure \(p_0\) and surface temperature \(T_0\). For Earth, \(H \approx 8.5\;\text{km}\). The initial integration step size is set to \(\Delta h = H/200\), giving about 200 steps per scale height.

Temperature Profile

Real atmospheres have non-uniform temperature structures driven by solar heating, convection, and radiation. The program accepts an arbitrary set of \((h_i,\,T_i)\) measurement pairs and constructs a piecewise temperature profile:

\[ T(h) = T_{i-1} + \frac{T_i - T_{i-1}}{h_i - h_{i-1}}\,(h - h_{i-1}), \qquad h_{i-1} \le h < h_i \tag{7.5} \]

That is, temperature is linearly interpolated between adjacent measured altitudes. Below the first measurement point the temperature is held constant at \(T_0\).

Upper Atmosphere Extension (Power-Law Model)

When the integration surpasses the highest measured altitude, the program switches to a power-law relationship between temperature and pressure. At the first step above the highest measurement point a coefficient \(\beta\) is fixed such that the profile is continuous:

\[ T = \beta\,p^{\,\alpha}, \qquad \alpha = 0.5 \tag{7.6} \]

The exponent \(\alpha = 0.5\) (the default power attribute) means that temperature falls as the square root of pressure. This is a simple empirical model that gives a monotonically decreasing temperature as the atmosphere thins, matching observations reasonably well for Earth above 50 km.

Algorithm

Finite-Difference Integration

Equation (7.1) is discretized using a first-order forward (Euler) step:

\[ p_j = p_{j-1} - g\,\rho_{j-1}\,\Delta h \tag{7.7} \]

At each altitude step the density \(\rho_{j-1}\) is obtained from the ideal gas law (Eq. 7.2) using the current pressure and the temperature profile at the current altitude. The integration terminates as soon as \(p_j < 0\), at which point the last valid step is discarded.

Adaptive Step-Size (dh Doubling)

The outer loop uses an adaptive strategy: if the inner for-loop of 1 000 steps does not bring the pressure to zero (i.e. the atmosphere has not been fully traversed), the step size \(\Delta h\) is doubled and the integration restarts from the surface. This ensures that very extended atmospheres — or those with very large scale heights — are always captured within a reasonable number of iterations. 

rho0  = ideal_gas_density(p0, mu, T0)      # surface density
scale = p0 / (g * rho0)                    # scale height H
dh    = scale / 200.0                      # initial step

while True:
    for j in range(1, 1001):
        p[j] = hydrostatic_step(p[j-1], rho[j-1], g, dh)
        T[j] = profile.get_temp(h[j], p[j])
        rho[j] = ideal_gas_density(p[j], mu, T[j])
        if p[j] < 0:
            last_step = j
            break                          # atmosphere top reached
    else:
        dh *= 2.0                          # double step, restart
        continue
    break                                  # done

Output Arrays

After the loop, the result arrays are trimmed to [:last_step], excluding the unphysical negative-pressure point. The extract_output() function then selects whichever quantity the user requested (pressure, density, or temperature) and packages it with axis labels, units, and a title for the plotter.

Numerical note: The first-order Euler scheme is adequate here because the exponential decrease in pressure means each step remains a small fraction of the scale height. For quantitative accuracy at large altitudes, a smaller initial dh (or higher-order integrator) could be substituted in driver_atmosphere.py without changing any other module.

Module: physics_atmosphere.py

Physics Layer

physics_atmosphere.py

Encodes the physical constants and the three primitive equations that govern atmospheric structure. No integration logic appears here — only pure physics. The only literal numbers are universal physical constants.

Physical Constants

K_BOLTZMANN = 1.38e-23   # Boltzmann constant  (J K⁻¹)
M_PROTON    = 1.67e-27   # Proton mass          (kg)

Data Class

@dataclass
class TemperatureProfile:
    h: List[float] # altitude breakpoints (m)
    T: List[float] # temperatures at breakpoints (K)
    power: float = 0.5 # exponent α in T = β·p^α
    beta: float = 0.0 # coefficient β (set on first pass above top)
    reached_top: bool = False

Stores the measured temperature profile and state for the upper-atmosphere power-law extension. reached_top is set True the first time the integration altitude exceeds the highest measured breakpoint, at which point beta is fixed and the power-law model takes over.

Functions

TemperatureProfile.get_temp(self, altitude: float, pressure: float) -> float

Returns the temperature at the given altitude and current pressure. Uses linear interpolation (Eq. 7.5) between breakpoints while inside the measured range; below the first breakpoint returns \(T_0\) (constant); above the highest breakpoint applies \(T = \beta\,p^{\alpha}\) (Eq. 7.6), fixing \(\beta\) on the first call in that regime so the profile is continuous.

ideal_gas_density(pressure: float, mu: float, temperature: float) -> float

Returns the mass density \(\rho = p\,\mu\,m_p\,/\,(k_B\,T)\) in kg m−3. Implements Eq. (7.2) directly. Requires pressure > 0 and temperature > 0.

hydrostatic_step(pressure_prev: float, rho_prev: float, g_accel: float, dh: float) -> float

Advances the pressure by one Euler step: \(p_j = p_{j-1} - g\,\rho_{j-1}\,\Delta h\). Implements Eq. (7.7). Negative results signal that the atmosphere top has been passed.

Module: driver_atmosphere.py

Driver / Solver

driver_atmosphere.py

Owns all integration logic: builds the temperature profile, computes the initial scale height, runs the while/for adaptive loop, and packages results into a structured output dictionary ready for the plotter.

Type Alias

OutputType = Literal["Pressure", "Density", "Temperature"]

Constrains output_type to one of the three supported output quantities.

Data Classes

@dataclass
class AtmosphereParameters:
    planet_name: str
    g_accel: float # gravitational acceleration (m s⁻²)
    mu: float # mean molecular weight (dimensionless)
    p0: float # surface pressure (Pa)
    h_points: List[float] # altitude breakpoints (m)
    T_points: List[float] # temperature at each breakpoint (K)
    output_type: OutputType

User-supplied configuration for one atmosphere model run.

@dataclass
class AtmosphereResult:
    altitudes: List[float]
    pressures: List[float]
    densities: List[float]
    temperatures: List[float]
    output_type: OutputType
    planet_name: str

Structured result from AtmosphereModel.run(). All four lists are co-indexed and trimmed to the last valid (non-negative-pressure) step.

Class

class AtmosphereModel:
    def __init__(self, params: AtmosphereParameters) -> None
    def run(self) -> AtmosphereResult

__init__ stores the parameters and constructs a TemperatureProfile from h_points and T_points. run() computes the initial density and scale height, then executes the adaptive while/for integration loop (described in the Algorithm section), returning an AtmosphereResult with arrays sliced to [:last_step].

Function

extract_output(result: AtmosphereResult) -> Dict

Converts an AtmosphereResult into a plot-ready dictionary with keys x, y, y_unit, x_label, y_label, and title. The y array and label depend on result.output_type: pressures (Pa), densities (kg m−3), or temperatures (K). The x array is always the altitude in meters.

Module: plot_atmosphere.py

Visualization

plot_atmosphere.py

Accepts the structured output dictionary from extract_output() and produces a matplotlib line graph. The module is intentionally minimal: all axis labels, units, and the title are already embedded in the dictionary, so the plotter needs no knowledge of the underlying physics.

Function

plot_atmosphere(curve_data: Dict[str, object]) -> None

Reads x (altitudes, m), y (selected quantity), x_label, y_label, and title from curve_data. Creates a single-axes figure with ax.plot(x, y), applies labels and a grid, then calls plt.tight_layout() and plt.show().

Module: main.py

Entry Point

main.py

The user-facing entry point. Sets all input parameters to Earth defaults and calls the driver and plotter. Edit the parameters in this file to model a different planet or atmospheric scenario; the other three modules never need to be modified. 

from driver_atmosphere import AtmosphereParameters, AtmosphereModel, extract_output
from plot_atmosphere  import plot_atmosphere

planet_name = "Earth"
g_accel     = 9.81          # m s⁻²
mu          = 28.97         # mean molecular weight (dry air)
p0          = 1.013e5       # Pa  (1 atm)
h_points    = [0.0, 2000.0, 6000.0, 12000.0, 25000.0, 50000.0]   # m
T_points    = [288.0, 275.0, 250.0, 225.0, 220.0, 270.0]          # K
output_type = "Pressure"    # "Pressure" | "Density" | "Temperature"

params = AtmosphereParameters(
    planet_name=planet_name, g_accel=g_accel, mu=mu, p0=p0,
    h_points=h_points, T_points=T_points, output_type=output_type
)

result     = AtmosphereModel(params).run()
curve_data = extract_output(result)
plot_atmosphere(curve_data)

Parameters

All parameters are set in main.py. The defaults model Earth's atmosphere using a six-point temperature profile drawn from the U.S. Standard Atmosphere.

Parameter Default (Earth) Unit Description
planet_name "Earth" — Label used in plot title
g_accel 9.81 m s−2 Surface gravitational acceleration
mu 28.97 dimensionless Mean molecular weight of atmosphere (dry air ≈ 28.97; CO₂ ≈ 44; H₂ ≈ 2)
p0 1.013e5 Pa Surface (reference) pressure (1 atm = 101 325 Pa)
h_points [0, 2000, 6000, 12000, 25000, 50000] m Altitude breakpoints of the temperature profile
T_points [288, 275, 250, 225, 220, 270] K Temperature at each altitude breakpoint
output_type "Pressure" — Which quantity to plot: "Pressure", "Density", or "Temperature"
Temperature profile note: The Earth defaults approximate the U.S. Standard Atmosphere 1976 — a troposphere that cools from 288 K at sea level to 225 K at 12 km (the tropopause), a stratosphere that warms to 270 K by 50 km, and a power-law extrapolation above that. The h_points and T_points lists must have the same length and must be in ascending altitude order.

Output Modes

Set output_type in main.py to select which atmospheric quantity is plotted on the vertical axis (with altitude on the horizontal axis).

"Pressure"
y-axis unit: Pa

Atmospheric pressure vs. altitude. Decreases roughly exponentially with a scale height of ~8.5 km for Earth. The most sensitive indicator of how extended the atmosphere is.

"Density"
y-axis unit: kg m−3

Mass density vs. altitude, derived from the ideal gas law. Also decreases exponentially, but the profile is additionally modulated by temperature variations through \(\rho \propto p/T\).

"Temperature"
y-axis unit: K

Temperature vs. altitude. Directly shows the user-supplied profile in the troposphere/stratosphere, and the \(T = \beta\,p^{0.5}\) power-law extension above the highest breakpoint.

Logarithmic scale tip: Pressure and density span many orders of magnitude. To see the full profile clearly, add ax.set_xscale('log') (or set_yscale, depending on axis orientation) in plot_atmosphere.py after the ax.plot() call.

Suggested Experiments

These investigations mirror those suggested by Schutz for the original Java program. Each can be run by editing only main.py.

1 · Earth's Standard Atmosphere

Run the program with the default Earth parameters. Plot Pressure, then Density, then Temperature in three separate runs. Compare the pressure profile to the exponential barometric formula \(p = p_0\,e^{-h/H}\) with \(H = 8.5\;\text{km}\). Note how the non-constant temperature causes deviations from a pure exponential.

output_type = "Pressure" # then "Density", then "Temperature" # all other parameters at their defaults

2 · Logarithmic Pressure Profile

Add a logarithmic x-axis to the plotter (ax.set_xscale('log')). On a log-pressure vs. altitude plot, an isothermal atmosphere produces a straight line. Identify the regions in Earth's atmosphere where temperature is approximately constant and the profile is therefore nearly linear on the log scale.

output_type = "Pressure" # add ax.set_xscale('log') in plot_atmosphere.py

3 · Mars

Mars has a thin CO₂ atmosphere. Its pressure profile falls off much more sharply than Earth's because of stronger gravity per unit of molecular weight. The scale height for Mars is about 11 km, but the much lower surface pressure (≈ 610 Pa) means the total column is tiny.

planet_name = "Mars" g_accel = 3.72 # m s⁻² mu = 44.0 # CO₂ p0 = 610.0 # Pa h_points = [0.0, 10000.0, 25000.0, 45000.0] # m T_points = [210.0, 170.0, 145.0, 130.0] # K

4 · Venus

Venus has a crushing CO₂ atmosphere with surface pressure ≈ 92 atm. Despite similar gravity to Earth, the enormous molecular weight and surface pressure create a very compact but extremely massive atmosphere. Compare its scale height to Earth's.

planet_name = "Venus" g_accel = 8.87 # m s⁻² mu = 44.0 # CO₂ p0 = 9.2e6 # Pa (~92 atm) h_points = [0.0, 10000.0, 30000.0, 60000.0] T_points = [735.0, 500.0, 340.0, 230.0] # K

5 · Jupiter (Upper Atmosphere)

Model the outer visible layer of Jupiter's hydrogen–helium atmosphere. Jupiter's scale height is enormous because of its low mean molecular weight (\(\mu \approx 2.2\)) despite its strong gravity. Use a reference level near the 1-bar cloud deck.

planet_name = "Jupiter" g_accel = 24.8 # m s⁻² mu = 2.2 # H₂/He mix p0 = 1.0e5 # Pa (1 bar reference level) h_points = [0.0, 20000.0, 50000.0, 100000.0] T_points = [165.0, 130.0, 110.0, 150.0] # K

6 · Varying Molecular Weight on Earth

Keep all Earth parameters fixed but change mu. How does the scale height change if Earth's atmosphere were pure water vapor (\(\mu = 18\)) or pure CO₂ (\(\mu = 44\))? Verify the theoretical prediction \(H \propto 1/\mu\).

mu = 18.0 # water vapor — compare to default 28.97 and to 44.0

7 · Effect of Temperature Profile Shape

Replace the six-point Earth profile with a simple two-point isothermal profile (h_points=[0, 100000], T_points=[288, 288]). The output should be very close to the barometric exponential. Then add a temperature inversion (warm layer aloft) and observe how it changes the density profile.

h_points = [0.0, 50000.0, 100000.0] T_points = [288.0, 200.0, 350.0] # inversion above 50 km

Related Programs

Atmosphere shares the same layered module architecture — physics / driver / plot / main — with all other programs in this suite. The programs most closely related in physical content are:

Star Neutron
  • Star (Chapter 8) — integrates the analogous Newtonian hydrostatic equilibrium equation for a self-gravitating polytropic star. The structure equation \(dp/dr = -G M(r)\,\rho/r^2\) is the stellar generalization of Eq. (7.1), with gravity varying with radius and mass continuity replacing the ideal-gas closure.
  • Neutron (Chapter 9) — extends stellar structure to general relativity via the Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff (TOV) equation, the relativistic counterpart of Atmosphere's hydrostatic step with a polytropic equation of state.
