Description

EarthOrbit is a numerical realisation of Newton's Cannon — the thought experiment by which Isaac Newton first explained orbital motion in the Principia (1687). A projectile is launched horizontally from a tower high above Earth's surface. If the launch speed is too low, the trajectory curves back to the ground. At exactly the right speed the curvature of the path matches the curvature of Earth and the projectile orbits indefinitely. Above that speed the orbit becomes an ellipse; above the escape speed it becomes a hyperbola and the projectile leaves Earth permanently.

The program integrates the projectile's two-dimensional trajectory using a fixed-timestep symplectic integrator, plotting the result against a reference circle showing Earth's surface. All the parameters — altitude, launch velocity, timestep, and step count — are set in main.py.

Textbook reference: Bernard Schutz, Gravity from the Ground Up, Cambridge University Press (2003), Chapter 4.

Physical Background

Newton's Cannon Thought Experiment

Newton imagined a cannon mounted on a mountain so high it clears the atmosphere, firing horizontally with increasing powder charges. The sequence of outcomes illustrates a continuous family of trajectories:

  • Sub-orbital arc: below the circular speed the trajectory curves more steeply than Earth's surface and the projectile impacts the ground.
  • Circular orbit: at the circular speed \(v_c\) the curvature of the trajectory exactly matches Earth's curvature. The projectile never lands.
  • Elliptical orbit: between \(v_c\) and the escape speed \(v_e\) the orbit is a closed ellipse with the launch point as one of the apsides.
  • Escape trajectory: at or above the escape speed \(v_e = \sqrt{2}\,v_c\) the projectile leaves Earth and does not return.

The Inverse-Square Force Law

At any distance \(r\) from Earth's centre the gravitational acceleration has magnitude

Gravitational acceleration (inverse-square law) — Eq. 1
\[ |\mathbf{a}| \;=\; g\!\left(\frac{R_\oplus}{r}\right)^{\!2} \;=\; \frac{k}{r^2}, \qquad k \;\equiv\; g\,R_\oplus^2 \]

where \(g = 9.8\) m s−2 is the measured surface value, \(R_\oplus = 6\,378\,200\) m is Earth's mean radius, and the composite constant \(k \equiv g\,R_\oplus^2 \approx 3.987\times10^{14}\) m³ s−2 is numerically equal to \(GM_\oplus\). The direction is always toward the centre.

Force-law correctness: A common porting error is to use a constant magnitude \(|\mathbf{a}| = g\) (direction toward the centre, magnitude fixed at 9.8 m s−2). This is not Newton's law. By Bertrand's theorem, only two central force laws produce closed orbits: \(F \propto r^{-2}\) (Newtonian gravity) and \(F \propto r\) (harmonic oscillator). A constant-magnitude force (\(F \propto r^0\)) causes the orbit to precess — it produces a rosette pattern instead of a closed ellipse for any eccentricity \(e > 0\). This precession is genuine physics of the wrong equation; reducing the timestep makes no difference. The corrected code uses r³ in the denominator and defines _K = G_SURFACE * R_EARTH**2.

Circular and Escape Speeds

At orbital radius \(r = R_\oplus + h\):

\[ v_c \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{k}{r}}, \qquad v_e \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{2k}{r}} \;=\; \sqrt{2}\,v_c \]

Governing Equations

Equations of Motion

The state vector is \((x,\,y,\,u,\,v)\) where \((x, y)\) is the projectile position in the equatorial plane (origin at Earth's centre) and \((u, v)\) the velocity. Newton's second law gives four coupled ODEs:

Equations of motion — Eq. 3
\[ \dot{x} = u, \quad \dot{y} = v, \qquad \dot{u} = a_x = -\frac{k\,x}{r^3}, \quad \dot{v} = a_y = -\frac{k\,y}{r^3} \] \[ r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}, \qquad k = g\,R_\oplus^2 \]

Initial Conditions

By convention the projectile starts on the positive \(y\)-axis (the "top" of the orbit):

Initial conditions — Eq. 4
\[ x_0 = 0, \quad y_0 = R_\oplus + h_0, \quad u_0 = u_\text{init}, \quad v_0 = v_\text{init} \]

A purely horizontal launch is achieved by setting \(u_\text{init} = 0\) and \(v_\text{init} \neq 0\). This places the launch point at an apsis (periapsis or apoapsis, depending on whether \(v_\text{init}\) is below or above \(v_c\)). Setting \(u_\text{init} \neq 0\) adds a radial velocity component and rotates the orbit's orientation.

Integration Algorithm

Averaged-Velocity Euler (Symplectic Leapfrog)

The driver uses a fixed-timestep integrator. At each step the velocity at the end of the interval is estimated first, and the position is then advanced using the average of the old and new velocities:

Integration step — Eq. 5
\[ u_1 = u_0 + a_x(x_0,y_0)\,\Delta t, \quad v_1 = v_0 + a_y(x_0,y_0)\,\Delta t \] \[ x \;\leftarrow\; x_0 + \tfrac{1}{2}(u_0 + u_1)\,\Delta t, \quad y \;\leftarrow\; y_0 + \tfrac{1}{2}(v_0 + v_1)\,\Delta t \]

This scheme is equivalent to the velocity Verlet / leapfrog method and is symplectic: it conserves a slightly modified energy exactly, so orbits neither spiral inward nor outward over arbitrarily long integrations. Unlike the plain (forward) Euler method it does not accumulate a secular energy drift.

Termination Conditions

After each step the driver evaluates \(r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\) and stops when either:

  • \(r < R_\oplus\) — the projectile has hit Earth's surface, or
  • the step counter reaches maxSteps — the budget is exhausted.

The trajectory arrays are trimmed to the number of steps actually taken before being returned.

Choosing dt and maxSteps: At the default dt = 0.4 s and launch speed ≈ 7 900 m s−1, one orbital period at 300 m altitude is roughly 5 060 s, requiring about 12 650 steps. The default maxSteps = 15 000 therefore covers slightly more than one orbit. For higher altitudes (larger radii) the period is longer; increase maxSteps proportionally, or shrink dt if higher positional accuracy is needed.

Module Overview

Physics Layer
physics_earthorbit.py

Defines Earth's physical constants and the inverse-square gravitational acceleration. The only literal numbers in this module are the two measured surface parameters \(g\) and \(R_\oplus\); the composite constant _K is derived from them. No integration logic appears here.

G_SURFACE = 9.8 # m/s² (surface gravity) R_EARTH = 6_378_200.0 # m (Earth's mean radius) _K = G_SURFACE * R_EARTH**2 # m³/s² (= GM_Earth) def compute_acceleration(x, y) -> (ax, ay) # ax = -_K * x / r³, ay = -_K * y / r³
Driver Layer
driver_earthorbit.py

Implements the fixed-step symplectic integration loop. Imports compute_acceleration and R_EARTH from the physics module. Builds the 401-point Earth-surface reference circle and advances the projectile until surface impact or maxSteps is reached.

from physics_earthorbit import compute_acceleration, R_EARTH def run_earth_orbit( h0 = 300.0, # initial altitude (m) uInit = 0.0, # initial x-velocity (m/s) vInit = 7900.0, # initial y-velocity (m/s) dt = 0.4, # timestep (s) maxSteps = 15000 # max integration steps ) -> (xs, ys, xEarth, yEarth)
Plot Layer
plot_earthorbit.py

Renders the trajectory and Earth's surface circle on a single equal-aspect matplotlib figure. Contains no physics or integration logic. Imports only matplotlib.

def plot_earth_orbit(xs, ys, xEarth, yEarth) # Blue dashed: Earth surface (xEarth, yEarth) # Red solid: Projectile trajectory (xs, ys) # Equal aspect, grid, legend # Title: "EarthOrbit — Attempting to Achieve Orbit"
Entry Point
main.py

The only file the user needs to edit. Set the launch parameters here, then run python main.py. The plot appears immediately.

from driver_earthorbit import run_earth_orbit from plot_earthorbit import plot_earth_orbit xs, ys, xEarth, yEarth = run_earth_orbit( h0=300.0, uInit=0.0, vInit=7900.0, dt=0.4, maxSteps=15000) plot_earth_orbit(xs, ys, xEarth, yEarth)

Parameters

All parameters are set as arguments to run_earth_orbit() in main.py. The defaults produce a nearly circular orbit just 300 m above Earth's surface — the idealised Newton's Cannon scenario.

Parameter Default Units Description
h0 300.0 m Initial altitude above Earth's surface. The starting position is \((0,\;R_\oplus + h_0)\). The default is 300 metres, not kilometres — just above the height of the Eiffel Tower, representing Newton's idealised mountain-top. For a realistic low-Earth orbit use h0 = 300_000.0.
uInit 0.0 m s−1 Initial x-velocity (radial component at launch). Zero gives a purely tangential launch, placing the start point at an apsis of the orbit. A non-zero value adds a radial component and rotates the orbit orientation.
vInit 7900.0 m s−1 Initial y-velocity (tangential component at launch). The circular speed at 300 m altitude is \(v_c \approx 7\,906\) m s−1; the default 7 900 m s−1 is marginally below circular, producing a very slightly sub-circular orbit that grazes the surface. Increase to ~7 910 m s−1 for a clean circular orbit at this altitude.
dt 0.4 s Fixed integration timestep. The symplectic integrator is stable for any \(\Delta t\), but positional accuracy improves with smaller values. Halving dt while doubling maxSteps gives the same simulated duration with twice the spatial resolution.
maxSteps 15000 — Maximum number of integration steps. At dt = 0.4 s this covers 6 000 s of simulated time — just over one orbital period at 300 m altitude (\(T \approx 5\,060\) s). Increase for longer simulations or higher orbits with longer periods.
Altitude units — common mistake: h0 is in metres. Setting h0 = 300 gives an altitude of 300 m (just above the surface), not 300 km. To simulate a realistic low-Earth orbit at the altitude of the International Space Station, use h0 = 408_000 (408 km = 408 000 m) with vInit ≈ 7\,670 m s−1.

Output

The program produces a single matplotlib figure with two overlaid curves on shared equal-aspect axes:

  • Blue dashed circle — Earth's surface, drawn as 401 evenly spaced points on a circle of radius \(R_\oplus\). This is pre-computed by the driver and passed to the plotter as xEarth, yEarth.
  • Red solid line — the projectile trajectory \((x_j, y_j)\) for all accepted steps, from the launch point until surface impact or step budget exhaustion.

The axis labels are "x (meters)" and "y (meters)". The aspect ratio is forced equal so that the Earth appears as a true circle and orbital ellipses are not distorted. A legend and grid are included; the title reads EarthOrbit — Attempting to Achieve Orbit.

The four arrays returned by run_earth_orbit() can also be used directly for custom analyses — for example, computing altitude vs. time, speed vs. time, or overlaying multiple trajectories launched at different speeds on the same figure.

Suggested Experiments

EXP-1

Find the Circular Speed

With a purely tangential launch at 300 m altitude, scan vInit until the red trajectory closes into a circle exactly matching the blue Earth circle. Compare your empirical value with the theoretical prediction \(v_c = \sqrt{k/r}\).

h0=300.0, uInit=0.0 vInit=7906.0 (scan from 7900)
EXP-2

Sub-orbital Arc

Reduce the launch speed well below circular. The trajectory should curve back and intersect the Earth-surface circle. Vary the speed and observe how the range increases — a numerical demonstration of Newton's Cannon at low powder charges.

h0=300.0, uInit=0.0 vInit=5000.0
EXP-3

Elliptical Orbit at LEO Altitude

Switch to a realistic 300 km altitude. Set the launch speed above the circular value to produce an ellipse with 300 km perigee and a higher apogee. Increase maxSteps to capture the full orbit.

h0=300_000.0, uInit=0.0 vInit=9000.0, maxSteps=50000
EXP-4

Approach the Escape Speed

At 300 km altitude, \(v_e \approx 10\,931\) m s−1. Set vInit just below this value and watch the orbit open to a very elongated ellipse. At or above \(v_e\) the trajectory never returns.

h0=300_000.0, uInit=0.0 vInit=10900.0, maxSteps=100000
EXP-5

Radial Launch Component

Keep the total launch speed fixed at \(v_c\) but split it between uInit (radial) and vInit (tangential). The orbit is no longer circular — its orientation rotates and its eccentricity depends on the fraction of radial velocity.

h0=300.0, vInit=6000.0 uInit=5000.0 # |v|≈7810 ≈ v_c
EXP-6

GEO Orbit

A geostationary orbit sits at 35 786 km altitude where \(v_c \approx 3\,075\) m s−1. Simulate this orbit and compare the orbital period with the expected 24 hours. You will need a much smaller timestep and many more steps.

h0=35_786_000.0, uInit=0.0 vInit=3075.0 dt=10.0, maxSteps=10000
EXP-7

Verify Kepler's Third Law

Run circular orbits at several altitudes and record the simulated period (step count × dt) each time. Confirm that \(T^2 \propto r^3\) — Kepler's third law — holds numerically across the full range from 300 m to 35 786 km.

h0 = 300, 300_000, 35_786_000 vInit = v_c at each altitude
EXP-8

Energy Conservation Check

Add a post-processing step in main.py to compute the specific energy \(\mathcal{E} = \tfrac{1}{2}(u^2+v^2) - k/r\) at every step and plot it vs. time. Confirm that the symplectic integrator keeps \(\mathcal{E}\) bounded without secular drift over many orbits.

Compute from xs, ys returned by driver # save velocities in driver first

Related Programs

  • Orbit — the general planetary orbit program (Chapter 4). Uses a full predictor-corrector with adaptive timestep rather than the fixed-step leapfrog; supports five output modes including energy and velocity hodograph. EarthOrbit is the pedagogically simpler version focused specifically on Newton's Cannon.
  • RelativisticOrbit — integrates the Schwarzschild geodesic in the equatorial plane of a non-rotating black hole (Chapter 8). Shares the same predictor-corrector integration core as Orbit but adds the GR correction term \(Q/r^2\) to the effective acceleration.
  • Binary — extends the Orbit integrator to a two-body system with mutual gravitational interaction (Chapter 4). Both bodies move under Newton's law; the predictor-corrector loop handles the coupled equations simultaneously.
  • Atmosphere — companion program (Chapter 7) covering the related problem of vertical hydrostatic equilibrium in a planetary atmosphere, where the same constant-g vs. inverse-square distinction arises.
