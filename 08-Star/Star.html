Description of Star

The Star program computes the internal structure of a star using Newtonian gravity and a polytropic equation of state. The star is assumed to be spherically symmetric and composed of a perfect fluid whose pressure p and density ρ are related by a power law: p ∝ ργ, where γ is the polytropic exponent. Central pressure, central temperature, and mean molecular weight are used to fix the proportionality constant via the ideal gas law.

Starting from the center, the program integrates outward in radius, enforcing hydrostatic equilibrium and mass conservation. It builds arrays of radius, pressure, density, temperature, and enclosed mass until the pressure falls to zero, which defines the stellar surface. The resulting model shows the strong contrast between central and surface conditions and illustrates how mass, radius, and composition are related for a given polytropic exponent.

The Python implementation follows Bernard Schutz’s original Java program from Gravity From the Ground Up, but is organized into four modules:

  • a physics module encoding the differential equations and physical constants
  • a driver module performing the radial integration and producing structured output
  • a plot module that turns the structured output into graphs
  • a main module that sets example input parameters and calls the driver and plot modules

Using Star

The Python version of Star is located at:
https://github.com/tomyee3/GFTGU-Programs/tree/main/Star

The program is divided conceptually into four modules:

  1. Physics module (for example, physics.py):
    Encodes the physical laws and constants used in the model:
    • Newton’s gravitational constant G
    • Boltzmann’s constant k
    • proton mass m_p
    • hydrostatic equilibrium: 
      dp/dr = - G * ρ(r) * m(r) / r²
    • mass conservation: 
      dm/dr = 4π r² ρ(r)
    • polytropic equation of state: 
      ρ = D * p^(1/γ)
      where D is determined from the central pressure and density.
    • ideal gas law at the center: 
      ρ_c = p_c * m_p * μ / (k * T_c)
    Only physical constants appear as literal numbers in this module; all other quantities are passed in as parameters.
  2. Driver module (for example, driver.py):
    Performs the radial integration from the center to the surface. It:
    • computes central density from p_c, T_c, and μ
    • derives the polytropic constant D and the reciprocal exponent 1/γ
    • chooses an initial radial step dr based on a pressure scale height
    • initializes arrays for radius, pressure, density, temperature, and mass
    • uses special starting formulas at the first non-zero radius to avoid singularities
    • steps outward in radius, updating: 
      r[j]   = r[j-1] + dr
p[j]   = p[j-1] - G * ρ[j-1] * m[j-1] * dr / r[j-1]²
m[j]   = m[j-1] + 4π r[j-1]² ρ[j-1] dr
ρ[j]   = D * p[j]^(1/γ)
T[j]   = (m_p * μ / k) * p[j] / ρ[j]
    • stops when the pressure becomes zero or negative, recording the index of the stellar surface
    • if the surface is not reached within the allocated number of steps, adjusts dr and repeats the integration
    The driver returns a structured object (for example, a dictionary) containing the arrays and the index of the surface.
  3. Plot module (for example, plot_star.py):
    Uses a plotting library such as Matplotlib to display:
    • pressure vs radius
    • density vs radius
    • temperature vs radius
    • enclosed mass vs radius
    The user can choose which quantity to plot by setting an output type string (for example, "pressure", "density", "temperature", or "mass"). Radius is always the horizontal axis; the chosen quantity is the vertical axis. For pressure and density, logarithmic vertical axes are often useful.
  4. Main module (main.py):
    Acts as the entry point for users. It sets example values for:
    • central pressure p_c (in pascals)
    • central temperature T_c (in kelvins)
    • mean molecular weight μ (dimensionless)
    • polytropic exponent γ
    • maximum number of radial steps
    • initial scale factor for choosing dr
    • output type (pressure, density, temperature, or mass)
    Users may overwrite these values to model different stars. The main module calls the driver to compute the structure and then passes the result to the plot module to generate the desired graph.

To run the program: 

python main.py

Depending on the selected output type, the program will produce one or more plots showing the internal structure of the star.

Suggestions for playing with Star

The Python version allows extensive experimentation with stellar models. Here are some suggestions:

  1. Solar model.
    Use the default values for central pressure, temperature, mean molecular weight, and polytropic exponent to construct a model of the Sun. Compare the radial profiles of pressure, density, temperature, and mass with the standard solar model in Chapter 8.
  2. Sensitivity to central conditions.
    Change the central temperature by 10% while keeping the polytropic exponent and composition fixed. Examine how the total mass and radius of the model change. Adjust the central pressure to recover the original mass and see how the radius responds.
  3. Red giant models.
    Build models with the same composition as the Sun but with a polytropic exponent close to γ = 4/3, appropriate for giant stars. Try to construct a model with the Sun’s mass but a radius 100 times larger, and then models with ten times the solar mass.
  4. Neutron star models.
    Use γ = 2 and a mean molecular weight μ = 1 to approximate a neutron star in Newtonian gravity. Adjust central pressure and temperature to obtain a radius of about 10 km and a mass of 1.4 solar masses. Compare these models with the fully relativistic ones discussed in the program Neutron.
  5. Scaling relations.
    For a fixed polytropic exponent and composition, build several models with different central pressures and temperatures. Check whether the resulting masses and radii obey the scaling relations derived in Investigation 8.6.

Understanding Star

The Star program is closely related to the atmospheric model in Atmosphere, but with two key differences:

  1. Variable gravity.
    Gravity cannot be treated as constant inside a star. At the center the gravitational acceleration is zero, while at the surface the entire mass of the star contributes. The program therefore replaces a constant g with: 
    g(r) = G * m(r) / r²
    where m(r) is the mass interior to radius r. Only the interior mass contributes, because a spherically symmetric shell of matter outside r exerts no net gravitational force at r.
  2. Polytropic equation of state.
    Unlike the Earth’s atmosphere, the temperature structure of a star cannot be specified directly. Instead, the program assumes a polytropic relation between pressure and density and uses the ideal gas law at the center to fix the proportionality constant. Central pressure, central temperature, and mean molecular weight determine the central density via: 
    ρ_c = p_c * m_p * μ / (k * T_c)
    From this, the program computes: 
    D = ρ_c / p_c^(1/γ)
    so that: 
    ρ = D * p^(1/γ)
    holds throughout the star. Temperature at each radius is then recovered from the ideal gas law.

The remaining aspects of the program—array management, stopping criteria, and plotting—are straightforward extensions of the techniques used in Atmosphere.

Suggested modifications

A useful extension of the present program would be to allow the equation of state to change from region to region. For example, the Sun has a convection zone where γ ≈ 4/3, while deeper layers have larger exponents. You could:

  • divide the star into two or three radial regions
  • assign different polytropic exponents and mean molecular weights to each region
  • ensure continuity of pressure and mass at the boundaries

Such a model would more accurately represent real stars while still using the same numerical framework.
