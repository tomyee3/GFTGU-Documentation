Physical Background

The Star program models the internal structure of a spherically symmetric star in Newtonian gravity using a polytropic equation of state. Starting from user-supplied central pressure and temperature, it integrates outward through the stellar interior—tracking pressure, density, temperature, and enclosed mass as functions of radius—until the pressure drops to zero at the stellar surface.

Real stars are held in mechanical equilibrium by the outward pressure gradient of their hot gas balancing the inward force of self-gravity. In the simplest treatment, the gas obeys two coupled laws: the ideal gas law, which relates pressure, density, and temperature; and a polytropic equation of state, which relates pressure and density through a power law \(\rho \propto p^{1/\gamma}\). Together, these two relations determine the temperature throughout the star without requiring an explicit treatment of energy generation or radiative transport.

A critical feature that distinguishes this program from simpler atmospheric models is that gravity is not constant. The gravitational acceleration at radius \(r\) is determined by the mass \(m(r)\) enclosed within that radius, following Newton's shell theorem: \(g(r) = G\,m(r)/r^2\). The hydrostatic equilibrium equation and the mass continuity equation must therefore be integrated simultaneously as the radius increases outward from the center.

The default parameters in main.py are calibrated to give a rough solar model: a central pressure \(p_c = 2.5 \times 10^{16}\) Pa, a central temperature \(T_c = 1.5 \times 10^7\) K, a mean molecular weight \(\mu = 0.61\) appropriate for a fully ionized hydrogen–helium plasma, and a polytropic index \(\gamma = 5/3\) corresponding to a non-relativistic ideal monatomic gas.

Governing Equations

Ideal Gas Law

The pressure of an ideal gas with mean molecular weight \(\mu\) (in units of the proton mass) is related to its mass density \(\rho\) and temperature \(T\) by

Ideal Gas Law  (1)
\[\begin{equation} p \;=\; \frac{\rho\, k_{\mathrm{B}}\, T}{m_p\, \mu} \label{eq:ideal-gas} \end{equation}\]

where \(k_{\mathrm{B}} = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}\) J/K is the Boltzmann constant and \(m_p = 1.67 \times 10^{-27}\) kg is the proton mass. It is convenient to define the combination

q Factor  (2)
\[\begin{equation} q \;=\; \frac{m_p\,\mu}{k_{\mathrm{B}}} \label{eq:q-factor} \end{equation}\]

so that the ideal gas law becomes \(p = \rho\,T/q\) and the temperature can be recovered at any point as \(T = q\,p/\rho\). The function q_factor(mu) in physics_star.py computes this combination; it is the only place where the values of \(k_{\mathrm{B}}\) and \(m_p\) appear in the code.

Polytropic Equation of State

A polytrope relates density to pressure through a power law parameterized by the adiabatic exponent \(\gamma\):

Polytropic EOS  (3)
\[\begin{equation} \rho \;=\; D\, p^{1/\gamma} \label{eq:polytrope} \end{equation}\]

The proportionality constant \(D\) is fixed by requiring the polytrope to reproduce the central density \(\rho_c\) at the central pressure \(p_c\):

Polytropic Constant  (4)
\[\begin{equation} D \;=\; \frac{\rho_c}{p_c^{1/\gamma}} \label{eq:poly-D} \end{equation}\]

Once \(D\) is determined from the central conditions, it is used at every subsequent radial step to obtain density from pressure, without any further reference to temperature. This makes the polytropic model self-contained: temperature is not an independent variable but a derived quantity recovered from \(T = q\,p/\rho\).

Hydrostatic Equilibrium

A star in mechanical equilibrium satisfies the condition that the outward pressure gradient exactly balances the inward gravitational force per unit volume:

Hydrostatic Equilibrium  (5)
\[\begin{equation} \frac{dp}{dr} \;=\; -\,\frac{G\,m(r)\,\rho}{r^2} \label{eq:hydrostatic} \end{equation}\]

where \(G = 6.672 \times 10^{-11}\) N m\(^2\) kg\(^{-2}\) is Newton's gravitational constant and \(m(r)\) is the mass enclosed within radius \(r\). The local gravitational acceleration \(g(r) = G\,m(r)/r^2\) varies with position, unlike the constant-\(g\) approximation used in atmosphere models.

Mass Continuity

The rate of change of enclosed mass with radius is the mass of a thin spherical shell of thickness \(dr\) and radius \(r\):

Mass Continuity  (6)
\[\begin{equation} \frac{dm}{dr} \;=\; 4\pi r^2\,\rho \label{eq:mass-cont} \end{equation}\]

Equations (5) and (6) are coupled: the pressure gradient depends on \(m(r)\), which in turn depends on the density distribution, which depends on the pressure through the polytrope (3). All four quantities—\(r\), \(p\), \(\rho\), \(m\)—must be advanced simultaneously at each radial step.

Initial Conditions

At the exact center of the star (\(r = 0\)), the hydrostatic equilibrium equation (5) is singular because \(r^2\) appears in the denominator. The integration therefore begins at the first radial grid point \(r_1 = dr\), a small but finite distance from the center. All initial values are derived from the user-supplied central pressure \(p_c\) and central temperature \(T_c\).

Central Density

The central density follows directly from the ideal gas law (1) evaluated at the center:

Central Density  (7)
\[\begin{equation} \rho_c \;=\; \frac{p_c\,q}{T_c} \label{eq:rho-c} \end{equation}\]

Pressure Scale Height

To choose an appropriate radial step size, the program estimates the characteristic length scale over which the pressure varies significantly near the center:

Pressure Scale Height  (8)
\[\begin{equation} \ell \;=\; \frac{\sqrt{p_c/G}}{\rho_c} \label{eq:scale-height} \end{equation}\]

This is approximately the distance over which the pressure falls by a factor of order 2 near the center. The radial step size is then

Radial Step Size  (9)
\[\begin{equation} dr \;=\; \frac{\ell}{400} \label{eq:dr} \end{equation}\]

Initial Array Values at \(r_1 = dr\)

At the first grid point, pressure and density are taken to be their central values. The enclosed mass is that of a small sphere of radius \(dr\) at uniform central density, and the temperature is recovered from the ideal gas law:

Initial Conditions  (10)
\[\begin{equation} p_1 = p_c, \quad \rho_1 = \rho_c, \quad m_1 = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi\,dr^3\,\rho_c, \quad T_1 = \frac{q\,p_c}{\rho_c} \label{eq:ic} \end{equation}\]

Integration Algorithm

The driver module advances the arrays \(\{r_j,\, p_j,\, \rho_j,\, T_j,\, m_j\}\) from index \(j = 1\) outward toward the stellar surface using a forward Euler finite-difference scheme. At each step \(j\) the following sequence of updates is applied, each delegated to the corresponding function in physics_star.py:

Step 1 — Pressure Update

The discrete form of hydrostatic equilibrium (5):

Finite-Difference Pressure Step  (11)
\[\begin{equation} p_j \;=\; p_{j-1} \;-\; \frac{G\,\rho_{j-1}\,m_{j-1}\,dr}{r_{j-1}^2} \label{eq:dp} \end{equation}\]

Step 2 — Mass Update

The enclosed mass grows by the shell mass between \(r_{j-1}\) and \(r_j\):

Finite-Difference Mass Step  (12)
\[\begin{equation} m_j \;=\; m_{j-1} \;+\; 4\pi\,r_{j-1}^2\,\rho_{j-1}\,dr \label{eq:dm} \end{equation}\]

Step 3 — Density and Temperature

Once the new pressure \(p_j\) is known, density is recovered from the polytropic relation (3) and temperature from the ideal gas law:

Density and Temperature Update  (13)
\[\begin{equation} \rho_j \;=\; D\,p_j^{1/\gamma}, \qquad T_j \;=\; \frac{q\,p_j}{\rho_j} \label{eq:rho-T-update} \end{equation}\]

Surface Detection

The integration loop runs until the pressure turns negative, signaling that the integration has passed through the stellar surface: 

if p[j] < 0:
    last_index = j
    break

The stellar radius is \(r_{j-1}\) (the last step with positive pressure) and the total stellar mass is \(m_{j-1}\). All arrays are trimmed to last_index before being returned to the plotting module.

Adaptive Step Size

The initial radial step \(dr = \ell/400\) is calibrated to resolve the steep central pressure gradient near the core. For some parameter combinations—especially low central pressures or small \(\gamma\)—the stellar radius may be very large and the integration may exhaust all max_points steps without the pressure ever reaching zero.

In that case, the driver automatically doubles the step size and restarts from the initial conditions: 

while surface not found:
    dr *= 2
    restart from r[1]=dr, p[1]=p_c, rho[1]=rho_c, ...

This doubling repeats as many times as necessary until the integration completes within max_points steps. The trade-off is accuracy: a coarser step introduces larger truncation errors. For the default solar parameters, no doubling is needed.

Note on accuracy: The forward Euler method accumulates truncation error of order \(O(dr)\) per step. For the default parameters the step is fine enough that the resulting stellar radius and mass are realistic to within a few percent. Changing to a dramatically different parameter regime may require experimenting with max_points to balance resolution against computation time.

Module Overview

The program is divided into four cooperating modules following a strict physics / driver / plot / main separation of concerns. This architecture keeps the physical equations isolated from the numerical integration logic and from the display code.

physics_star.py
Physics

Encodes the physical laws of polytropic stellar structure as pure, stateless functions. Contains exactly three literal numbers—the physical constants \(k_{\mathrm{B}}\), \(m_p\), and \(G\)—and seven functions implementing the ideal gas law, polytropic EOS, hydrostatic equilibrium, and mass continuity. No integration logic resides here.

k_BOLTZMANN = 1.38e-23 # J/K
MPROTON = 1.67e-27 # kg
G_NEWTON = 6.672e-11 # m³ kg⁻¹ s⁻²

q_factor(mu)
central_density(p_c, T_c, mu)
polytropic_D(rho_c, p_c, gamma)
scale_height(p_c, rho_c)
hydrostatic_step(p_prev, rho_prev,
                mass_prev, r_prev, dr)
mass_step(mass_prev, r_prev, rho_prev, dr)
density_from_pressure(p, D, gamma)
temperature_from_prho(p, rho, mu)
driver_star.py
Driver

Orchestrates the full radial integration from center to surface. Computes central conditions and the initial step size, runs the forward-Euler loop calling physics_star functions at each step, applies step doubling if the surface is not reached within max_points steps, and returns all profiles in a structured dictionary.

integrate_star(
  p_c,
  T_c,
  mu,
  gamma,
  max_points = 2000,
  output_type = "pressure"
) -> dict

Returns keys:
  "radius", "pressure", "density",
  "temperature", "mass",
  "last_index", "output_type"
plot_star.py
Plot

Receives the result dictionary from the driver and produces a labelled matplotlib graph of the chosen quantity versus radius. Selects axis labels and title automatically from output_type. Raises ValueError for any unrecognized output type string.

plot_star_structure(result: Dict)

output_type → y-axis label
"pressure" → "Pressure [Pa]"
"density" → "Density [kg/m³]"
"temperature"→ "Temperature [K]"
"mass" → "Enclosed mass [kg]"

x-axis: "Radius [m]"
Title: "Stellar structure: <type> vs radius"
main.py
Entry Point

The user-facing entry script. Sets all physical input parameters to solar-model defaults and calls the driver and plotting modules in sequence. To explore a different stellar model, edit the parameter values here; no other file needs to change.

p_c = 2.5e16 # central pressure [Pa]
T_c = 1.5e7 # central temperature [K]
mu = 0.61 # mean molecular weight
gamma = 5.0/3.0 # polytropic exponent
output_type = "pressure"

result = integrate_star(
  p_c=p_c, T_c=T_c, mu=mu,
  gamma=gamma,
  max_points=2000,
  output_type=output_type)
plot_star_structure(result)

Input Parameters

All parameters are set in main.py. The defaults produce a rough model of the Sun. Change only the values in main.py; the physics, driver, and plot modules require no modification.

Parameter Default Units Description
p_c 2.5e16 Pa Central pressure. Sets the overall energy scale of the stellar model. Higher \(p_c\) generally produces a more massive, more compact star.
T_c 1.5e7 K Central temperature. Combined with \(p_c\) and \(\mu\) via the ideal gas law to set the central density \(\rho_c\). A higher \(T_c\) yields a lower \(\rho_c\) for the same \(p_c\), producing a larger, less dense star.
mu 0.61 dimensionless Mean molecular weight in units of the proton mass. For a fully ionized hydrogen–helium solar plasma, \(\mu \approx 0.61\). For a pure neutron fluid, \(\mu = 1\). Smaller \(\mu\) means lighter particles and higher pressure per unit density.
gamma 5.0/3.0 dimensionless Polytropic exponent \(\gamma\). The value \(5/3\) corresponds to a non-relativistic ideal monatomic gas. Red giants require \(\gamma\) closer to \(4/3\); neutron stars modeled with a stiff EOS use \(\gamma \approx 2\). Values near \(1\) produce very extended, low-density stars.
max_points 2000 integer Maximum number of radial grid points per integration attempt. If the stellar surface is not reached, the step size doubles and integration restarts. Increase this value for better resolution with extreme parameter choices.
output_type "pressure" string Selects the quantity displayed in the plot. One of "pressure", "density", "temperature", or "mass". All four profiles are always computed; only the display changes.
Physical meaning of \(\mu\): For a pure hydrogen plasma (fully ionized), each proton contributes one proton mass and one electron mass to the mean, but the electron mass is negligible, so \(\mu \approx 0.5\). For a solar-composition hydrogen–helium mixture (roughly 75% H, 25% He by mass, both fully ionized), \(\mu \approx 0.61\). For a pure neutron star modeled in Newtonian gravity, \(\mu = 1\) (no free electrons).

Output Modes

Set output_type in main.py to choose which stellar structure profile is plotted. In all modes the horizontal axis is the radius \(r\) in metres, running from the first grid point \(r = dr\) to the stellar surface. All four profiles are computed internally during each run; only the displayed quantity changes.

output_type Quantity plotted Units What to look for
"pressure" Pressure \(p(r)\) Pa Peaks at the center and falls monotonically to zero at the surface. The radius at which \(p \to 0\) is the stellar radius \(R_\star\).
"density" Density \(\rho(r)\) kg m\(^{-3}\) Also highest at the center, falling to zero at the surface. Because \(\rho \propto p^{1/\gamma}\) with \(\gamma > 1\), density falls off more slowly than pressure.
"temperature" Temperature \(T(r)\) K Hottest at the center. Since \(T = qp/\rho\) and \(\rho\) falls more slowly than \(p\), the temperature drops faster than pressure but more slowly than a simple linear interpolation would suggest.
"mass" Enclosed mass \(m(r)\) kg Increases from nearly zero at the center to the total stellar mass \(M_\star\) at the surface. The curve rises steeply in the dense interior and flattens near the low-density outer layers.

Suggested Scenarios

The following experiments are drawn from Schutz's original program description and the exercises in Gravity from the Ground Up, Chapter 8. Each requires only changes to the parameter values in main.py.

1 · Solar Model Baseline

Run with default parameters. Switch output_type between all four modes to build up a complete picture of the solar interior. Verify that the stellar radius is of order \(7 \times 10^8\) m and the total enclosed mass is of order \(2 \times 10^{30}\) kg.

p_c = 2.5e16, T_c = 1.5e7
mu = 0.61, gamma = 5.0/3.0
output_type = "mass"

2 · Central Temperature Sensitivity

Change \(T_c\) by ±10% while keeping \(p_c\) fixed. A higher central temperature lowers the central density (ideal gas law), making the star larger and less massive. Observe how sensitively the stellar radius and total mass respond to this small change.

T_c = 1.35e7 # −10%
T_c = 1.65e7 # +10%
output_type = "mass"

3 · Red Giant Model

Set \(\gamma\) close to \(4/3\) to model a gas that is close to the instability threshold. Near-\(4/3\) polytropes produce very extended, tenuous envelopes with radii roughly 100 times larger than a solar-type star. Compare the density profile with the baseline run.

gamma = 1.335
output_type = "density"

4 · Neutron Star (Newtonian)

Set \(\mu = 1\) (pure neutrons, no free electrons) and \(\gamma = 2\) (stiff nuclear matter). This gives a much more compact object with a much smaller radius. Note that a fully rigorous neutron-star model requires general relativity, as treated in the companion Neutron program.

mu = 1.0, gamma = 2.0
p_c = 2.5e16
output_type = "pressure"

5 · Scaling Relations

For a polytrope, dimensional analysis predicts that stellar radius \(R\) and mass \(M\) scale as power laws in \(p_c\) and \(\rho_c\). Vary \(p_c\) over a factor of 10 and plot \(\log R\) versus \(\log p_c\) to measure the exponent. Compare with the theoretical prediction for \(\gamma = 5/3\).

p_c in [1e15, 1e16, 1e17]
gamma = 5.0/3.0
output_type = "mass"

6 · Effect of Composition (\(\mu\))

Compare models with \(\mu = 0.5\) (pure hydrogen plasma), \(\mu = 0.61\) (solar), and \(\mu = 1.0\) (pure protons or neutrons). Higher \(\mu\) means heavier particles, higher central density at the same pressure and temperature, and a more compact star.

mu in [0.5, 0.61, 1.0]
p_c = 2.5e16, T_c = 1.5e7
output_type = "mass"
