Overview

MercPert simulates the trajectory of a Mercury-like test body orbiting under the gravitational influence of a central binary system — a Sun-like star and a massive planetary companion in mutual circular orbit. The simulation is a three-body Newtonian problem: the two binary members move on prescribed circular paths (their mutual orbit is computed analytically) while Mercury's trajectory is integrated numerically step by step.

The program is designed to reproduce and extend the perturbation experiments described in Schutz's A First Course in General Relativity. By varying the companion mass and orbital separation, you can watch Mercury's nearly Keplerian ellipse precess, deform, or become chaotic — purely due to Newtonian three-body effects. This provides a baseline against which general-relativistic precession effects can be compared using the companion program MercPert_RK45.

Scope: MercPert is a purely Newtonian simulation. No post-Newtonian or general-relativistic corrections are applied. The binary members move on analytically prescribed circular orbits; their back-reaction on each other from Mercury is neglected (Mercury is treated as a true test particle).

Quick Start

Requirements

Python 3.9 or later with matplotlib:

pip install matplotlib

Running the Program

cd GFTGU-Programs/MercPert
python main.py

The default configuration places a Sun-like star (\(M_\odot = 1.98847\times10^{30}\,\mathrm{kg}\)) and a super-Jupiter companion (0.1 solar masses, roughly 100 Jupiter masses) in a circular mutual orbit at 0.7 AU separation. Mercury starts at 1.0 AU from the barycentre with a purely transverse velocity of \(15{,}000\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\). The integration runs for up to 150,000 accepted steps, recording all three bodies' positions at each step and producing an orbital plot when finished.

Customising the Simulation

All initial conditions and run parameters are set near the top of main.py. The three configuration blocks are:

  • BinarySystemParams — companion mass and binary separation
  • MercuryInitialConditions — Mercury's starting position and velocity
  • MercPertRunParams — timestep, step limit, and tolerances

No other file needs editing for typical use.

Program Files

main.py
Entry Point & Configuration

Defines all initial conditions and run parameters, then calls run_mercpert() to integrate the trajectory and plot_orbits() to display the result. All user-adjustable quantities live here. The default example configures a Sun–super-Jupiter binary at 0.7 AU with Mercury starting at 1.0 AU with a 15,000 m s−1 transverse kick.

physics_mercpert.py
Physics Engine

Defines the physical constants (\(G\), \(M_\odot\), \(\mathrm{AU}\)) and the two dataclasses BinarySystemParams and MercuryInitialConditions. Provides five functions: k_gravity() computes the Keplerian gravitational parameter \(k = G(M_\odot + M_p)\); compute_binary_radii() returns the orbital radii of both binary members about their common centre of mass; compute_binary_angular_velocity() returns \(\omega\) from Kepler's third law; binary_positions(t) evaluates the Sun's and planet's Cartesian positions at time \(t\) on their circular orbits; and mercury_acceleration(t, x, y) evaluates the total Newtonian gravitational acceleration on Mercury due to both binary members at its current position.

driver_mercpert.py
Integration Driver

Defines MercPertRunParams, MercPertOutput, and the main integration function run_mercpert(). The driver advances Mercury's position and velocity using an Euler predictor followed by an iterated averaged-acceleration corrector, with fractional-change convergence testing on both position and velocity. A timestep-halving rule reduces dt whenever the accepted fractional change in any state variable exceeds \(\varepsilon_1\). Integration runs until max_steps accepted steps have been taken — there is no orbit-closure termination criterion in this program. At each accepted step the Sun's and planet's positions are also recorded via binary_positions().

plot_mercpert.py
Visualisation

Provides plot_orbits(output, title), which renders a single 6 × 6 inch matplotlib figure showing the trajectories of all three bodies in the \(xy\)-plane with equal aspect ratio: the Sun in red, the binary companion in green, and Mercury in blue. Axis labels use scientific notation and the plot title is set by the caller.

Three-Body Setup

The system contains three gravitationally interacting bodies:

  • The Sun — a central star of mass \(M_\odot\) specified via m_sun_solar in solar-mass units.
  • The Planet — a massive companion of mass \(M_p\) specified via m_planet_solar in solar-mass units.
  • Mercury — a test particle whose trajectory is integrated numerically. Its mass is negligible; it exerts no force on either binary member.

The Sun and Planet are treated as moving on analytically prescribed circular orbits about their common centre of mass. This approximation is valid when Mercury's mass is negligible compared to the binary members. It decouples the binary's motion from Mercury's trajectory, making the problem tractable as a driven one-body integration rather than a full three-body integration.

Centre of mass convention: The centre of mass of the Sun–Planet binary is placed at the origin. Both bodies orbit this point in the \(xy\)-plane. Mercury's initial conditions are specified relative to this same origin.

Binary Motion

Given binary separation \(d\) (the distance between the two binary members), Kepler's third law gives the angular velocity of the mutual circular orbit:

(1) \[ \omega \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{G\,(M_\odot + M_p)}{d^3}} \]

Each body orbits the centre of mass at its own radius. The Sun's orbital radius and the Planet's orbital radius satisfy:

(2) \[ r_\odot \;=\; \frac{M_p}{M_\odot + M_p}\,d, \qquad r_p \;=\; \frac{M_\odot}{M_\odot + M_p}\,d \]

so that \(r_\odot + r_p = d\) and the centre of mass remains at the origin. The Cartesian positions of both bodies at time \(t\) are then:

(3) \[ \mathbf{r}_\odot(t) \;=\; r_\odot \begin{pmatrix} \cos\omega t \\ \sin\omega t \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{r}_p(t) \;=\; -r_p \begin{pmatrix} \cos\omega t \\ \sin\omega t \end{pmatrix} \]

These are evaluated by binary_positions(t, binary_params) in physics_mercpert.py at every step of the integration, and their values are recorded alongside Mercury's position in MercPertOutput.

Mercury's Equation of Motion

Mercury experiences a Newtonian gravitational acceleration from both binary members simultaneously. Let \(\mathbf{r} = (x, y)\) be Mercury's position, and define the displacement vectors from Mercury to each binary body:

(4) \[ \boldsymbol{\Delta}_\odot \;=\; \mathbf{r}_\odot(t) - \mathbf{r}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\Delta}_p \;=\; \mathbf{r}_p(t) - \mathbf{r} \]

with scalar separations \(s_\odot = |\boldsymbol{\Delta}_\odot|\) and \(s_p = |\boldsymbol{\Delta}_p|\). Mercury's total acceleration is the vector sum of the two gravitational pulls:

(5) \[ \ddot{\mathbf{r}} \;=\; \frac{G\,M_\odot}{s_\odot^3}\,\boldsymbol{\Delta}_\odot \;+\; \frac{G\,M_p}{s_p^3}\,\boldsymbol{\Delta}_p \]

In component form, separating the \(x\)- and \(y\)-directions, the mercury_acceleration(t, x, y, binary_params) function returns the pair \((a_x, a_y)\) for use at every stage of the predictor–corrector loop.

Time-dependent forcing: Because the binary positions \(\mathbf{r}_\odot(t)\) and \(\mathbf{r}_p(t)\) rotate with angular velocity \(\omega\), Mercury's equation of motion is a non-autonomous second-order ODE. The acceleration at position \(\mathbf{r}\) depends not only on \(\mathbf{r}\) but also on the current time \(t\). The corrector must therefore evaluate the acceleration at \(t + \Delta t\) (not at \(t\)) when computing the predicted positions.

Initial Conditions & Orbits

Mercury's starting state is specified by the four quantities in MercuryInitialConditions: its initial \(x\)- and \(y\)-position (\texttt{x\_init}, \texttt{y\_init}) and its initial velocity components (\texttt{vx\_init}, \texttt{vy\_init}), all in SI units (metres and m s−1).

Unlike the two-body Binary program, there is no conserved energy diagnostic in MercPert: because the binary members move on externally imposed orbits, the binary's potential energy is not part of the integration, and the total mechanical energy of the three-body system is not conserved by the algorithm. Instead, the quality of the integration is assessed visually (smooth, non-crossing orbits for nearly Keplerian configurations) and by tightening eps1 and eps2 until the trajectory no longer changes perceptibly.

Choosing Initial Conditions

A purely transverse initial velocity (\texttt{vx\_init} = 0, \texttt{vy\_init} > 0) with Mercury starting on the positive \(x\)-axis produces an orbit that is nearly elliptical when the companion mass is small. For a circular orbit of radius \(r_0\) around a single body of mass \(M_\odot\) the required circular velocity is:

(6) \[ v_c \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{G\,M_\odot}{r_0}} \]

Setting \texttt{vy\_init} close to \(v_c\) produces a nearly circular baseline orbit whose deviations under the companion's perturbation are then easy to see.

Tip: For \(r_0 = 1\,\mathrm{AU}\) and \(M_\odot = 1.98847\times10^{30}\,\mathrm{kg}\), equation (6) gives \(v_c \approx 29{,}780\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\) — very close to Mercury's mean orbital speed. The default \texttt{vy\_init} = 15,000 m s−1 is intentionally sub-circular in order to produce a clearly elliptical reference orbit.

Predictor–Corrector

The integration scheme is a two-stage predictor–corrector. Let superscript \(n\) denote values at the current time \(t\) and \(n+1\) the values to be determined at \(t + \Delta t\). The acceleration at the current state is written \(\mathbf{a}^n = \mathbf{a}(t, \mathbf{r}^n)\).

Stage 1 — Euler Predictor

An explicit Euler step provides first estimates of velocity and position at \(t + \Delta t\):

(7) \[ \tilde{\mathbf{v}} \;=\; \mathbf{v}^n + \mathbf{a}^n\,\Delta t, \qquad \tilde{\mathbf{r}} \;=\; \mathbf{r}^n + \mathbf{v}^n\,\Delta t \]

The acceleration at the predicted state is then evaluated using the binary positions at the new time \(t + \Delta t\): \(\tilde{\mathbf{a}} = \mathbf{a}(t + \Delta t,\, \tilde{\mathbf{r}})\).

Stage 2 — Averaged-Acceleration Corrector

The corrector refines both the velocity and the position by averaging accelerations and velocities across the step:

(8) \[ \mathbf{v}^{n+1} \;=\; \mathbf{v}^n + \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(\mathbf{a}^n + \tilde{\mathbf{a}}\right)\Delta t \]
(9) \[ \mathbf{r}^{n+1} \;=\; \mathbf{r}^n + \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(\mathbf{v}^n + \mathbf{v}^{n+1}\right)\Delta t \]

Corrector Iteration

After the first corrector pass, the acceleration is re-evaluated at the new position and time, and the corrector equations (8)–(9) are applied again using the updated acceleration. The loop continues for up to 10 iterations, exiting early when the maximum fractional change in all four state variables \((v_x, v_y, x, y)\) between successive iterations falls below \(\varepsilon_2\):

(10) \[ \max\!\left( \frac{|v_x^{(k+1)} - v_x^{(k)}|}{|v_x^{(k+1)}|},\; \frac{|v_y^{(k+1)} - v_y^{(k)}|}{|v_y^{(k+1)}|},\; \frac{|x^{(k+1)} - x^{(k)}|}{|x^{(k+1)}|},\; \frac{|y^{(k+1)} - y^{(k)}|}{|y^{(k+1)}|} \right) \;<\; \varepsilon_2 \]
Difference from Binary: The MercPert corrector tests fractional convergence on both positions and velocities, whereas Binary tests only accelerations. This is because Mercury's equation of motion is non-autonomous (explicitly time-dependent): position errors propagate directly into the force evaluation at the next corrector iteration.

Adaptive Timestep

After the corrector loop converges, the driver evaluates how large the state changes were over the accepted step. The maximum fractional change across all four state variables is compared to \(\varepsilon_1\):

(11) \[ \delta \;=\; \max\!\left( \frac{|v_x^{n+1} - v_x^n|}{|v_x^{n+1}|},\; \frac{|v_y^{n+1} - v_y^n|}{|v_y^{n+1}|},\; \frac{|x^{n+1} - x^n|}{|x^{n+1}|},\; \frac{|y^{n+1} - y^n|}{|y^{n+1}|} \right) \]

Step Rejection & Timestep Halving

If \(\delta > \varepsilon_1\), the step is rejected. The working timestep is halved and the entire predictor–corrector cycle is retried from the unchanged state at time \(t\):

(12) \[ \Delta t \;\leftarrow\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\Delta t \qquad (\delta > \varepsilon_1) \]

If \(\delta \le \varepsilon_1\), the step is accepted, \(t\) is advanced by \(\Delta t\), and the state is updated. The working timestep is not grown after an accepted step in this program — unlike Binary, MercPert uses a halving-only adaptive strategy. The timestep can therefore only decrease from its initial value over the course of the run, concentrating steps wherever the trajectory changes rapidly (near closest approach to either binary member).

No timestep growth: This differs from the Binary program, which grows the timestep by 10% after each accepted step. The simpler halving-only strategy in MercPert is more conservative and may produce more steps than strictly necessary in quiet phases of the orbit, but avoids any risk of the timestep growing too large when returning to a previously quiet region.

Convergence Criteria

Two independent tolerance parameters control integration quality. Both are set in main.py via MercPertRunParams.

Parameter Symbol Default Role
eps1 \(\varepsilon_1\) \(0.05\) Adaptive timestep gate. Steps whose maximum fractional state change \(\delta\) exceeds this value are rejected and retried at half the timestep. A smaller value forces shorter timesteps and higher accuracy throughout.
eps2 \(\varepsilon_2\) \(0.01\) Corrector convergence test. The corrector loop exits when the fractional change in every state variable between successive corrector iterations falls below this value (or after 10 iterations). Should be strictly less than \(\varepsilon_1\).
Design relationship: \(\varepsilon_2\) should always be smaller than \(\varepsilon_1\) so that the corrector fully converges before the timestep controller evaluates \(\delta\). The defaults \(\varepsilon_1 = 0.05\), \(\varepsilon_2 = 0.01\) satisfy this. The defaults are more permissive than those in Binary because MercPert runs for many more steps (up to 150,000) and the perturbation structure is more important than absolute trajectory precision.

Output

The program produces a single orbital plot via plot_orbits(output, title). The figure is a 6 × 6 inch matplotlib window with equal aspect ratio, showing:

  • Red — the Sun's trajectory (a small circle around the centre of mass)
  • Green — the binary companion's trajectory (a larger circle, opposite the Sun)
  • Blue — Mercury's trajectory (the orbit of interest)

The underlying data is returned from run_mercpert() as a MercPertOutput dataclass containing seven time-series lists: times, sun_x, sun_y, planet_x, planet_y, merc_x, merc_y. These can be used directly for custom analysis or additional plots beyond the built-in orbital view.

Extending the output: MercPert currently supports only the orbital trajectory plot. To add velocity-space, position-vs.-time, or energy-budget plots, extend MercPertOutput to store velocity time-series in driver_mercpert.py and add the corresponding plot functions to plot_mercpert.py.
