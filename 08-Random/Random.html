Program Description

Random simulates three-dimensional random walks and measures how far a randomly wandering particle travels from its starting point as a function of the number of steps taken. The simulation is motivated by one of astrophysics' most striking transport phenomena: the extraordinarily slow journey of a photon from the core of the Sun to its surface.

A photon produced by nuclear fusion at the Sun's center does not travel outward in a straight line. Instead, it is absorbed and re-emitted by the dense solar plasma billions of times, changing direction randomly at each interaction. The net outward displacement after \(N\) such steps is not proportional to \(N\) — as it would be for directed motion — but rather to \(\sqrt{N}\). This square-root scaling is the hallmark of diffusion, and it means that a photon requires roughly 10,000 to 170,000 years to traverse the 700,000 km from core to surface, even though light itself crosses that distance in about 2.3 seconds if unobstructed.

The program performs a systematic suite of experiments. Starting with a maximum walk length of maxSteps, it halves the number of steps repeatedly, running nTrials independent random walks at each step-count to obtain a statistically reliable average. The result is a table — and a log-log plot — of average scaled net displacement versus number of steps. On log-log axes a diffusive process appears as a straight line with slope exactly \(\tfrac{1}{2}\), confirming the \(\langle d \rangle \propto \sqrt{N}\) scaling law.

Each step in a single walk has three components drawn independently and uniformly from the interval \([-1, +1]\). This mimics an isotropic scattering event where direction is equally likely in any direction. Because the step lengths vary (a step drawn near a corner of the unit cube is longer than one drawn near a face), the raw displacement is normalized by the average step length before averaging across trials, producing a dimensionless scaled distance that cleanly reveals the diffusive scaling.

Astrophysical context: The random walk is the simplest model of radiative diffusion in stellar interiors. More realistic models account for the frequency-dependent mean free path of photons and the temperature gradient driving a net outward energy flux, but the \(\sqrt{N}\) scaling persists and governs the overall timescale of energy transport.

Physics & Equations

The Random Step

At each step \(i\), three displacements are drawn independently and uniformly from \([-1, +1]\):

(1) \[ \Delta x_i = 2 U_i^{(x)} - 1, \quad \Delta y_i = 2 U_i^{(y)} - 1, \quad \Delta z_i = 2 U_i^{(z)} - 1, \qquad U \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0,1). \]

Individual Step Length

The Euclidean length of step \(i\) is:

(2) \[ \ell_i = \sqrt{\Delta x_i^2 + \Delta y_i^2 + \Delta z_i^2}. \]

Because each component is uniform on \([-1,1]\), the expected step length is \(\langle \ell \rangle = \sqrt{3}/2 \approx 0.866\), but individual values range from \(0\) to \(\sqrt{3}\).

Accumulated Position and Net Displacement

After \(N\) steps the walker's position is:

(3) \[ X = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \Delta x_i, \quad Y = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \Delta y_i, \quad Z = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \Delta z_i. \]
(4) \[ D = \sqrt{X^2 + Y^2 + Z^2}. \]

Average Step Length and Scaled Distance

To compare walks of different step sizes on equal footing, the net displacement is divided by the mean step length accumulated during that walk:

(5) \[ \bar{\ell} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \ell_i. \]
(6) \[ d = \frac{D}{\bar{\ell}}. \]

Diffusion Scaling Law

For an unbiased random walk in three dimensions the expected scaled distance grows as the square root of the number of steps:

(7) \[ \langle d \rangle \propto \sqrt{N}. \]

On a log-log plot this appears as a straight line with slope \(\tfrac{1}{2}\):

(8) \[ \log \langle d \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2} \log N + \mathrm{const}. \]

The driver performs \(n_{\mathrm{walks}}\) experiments at geometrically spaced step counts. The number of experiments is determined by how many times maxSteps can be halved before reaching unity:

(9) \[ n_{\mathrm{walks}} = \left\lfloor \log_2(\texttt{maxSteps}) \right\rfloor. \]

For the default maxSteps = 4096 = 2^{12} this yields \(n_{\mathrm{walks}} = 12\) experiments with step counts \(4096, 2048, 1024, \ldots, 4, 2\).

Algorithm Overview

The simulation uses three nested levels of iteration, mirroring the structure of Schutz's original Java program:

  1. Outer loop — experiment suite: Iterates over walk lengths in descending order. Index \(j\) runs from \(n_{\mathrm{walks}}-1\) down to \(0\); the step count \(n_{\mathrm{steps}}\) starts at maxSteps and is integer-halved after each experiment. The loop exits early if \(n_{\mathrm{steps}} \le 1\).
  2. Middle loop — trial averaging: For each step count, nTrials independent walks are performed and their scaled distances are accumulated, then divided by nTrials to yield \(\langle d \rangle\) for that step count.
  3. Inner loop — single walk: Steps through \(n_{\mathrm{steps}}\) iterations, drawing random components, accumulating the running position \((x, y, z)\) and total step length step_size_total.
Why descending order? The outer loop fills lengths[j] and avgDist[j] with \(j\) running from high to low, so that upon return the arrays are in ascending order of step count — ready for plotting without any sorting.

After all experiments are complete, the driver returns two parallel lists: lengths (number of steps per experiment) and avgDist (mean scaled distance per experiment). The plot module renders these on log-log axes, where diffusive \(\sqrt{N}\) scaling manifests as a line of slope \(\tfrac{1}{2}\).

Module Structure

The program is divided into four Python modules following the standard GFtGU pattern: physics, driver, plot, and main. Each module has a single, well-defined responsibility.

random_physics.py

Contains all physics: random step generation, displacement accumulation, step-length normalization. No I/O, no plotting, no driver logic. The only numbers allowed are physical constants (none needed here — the walk is dimensionless).

perform_single_walk(n_steps) → (net_distance, avg_step_length) perform_trials(n_steps, n_trials) → avg_scaled_distance

perform_single_walk executes one 3D walk and returns the net displacement \(D\) and the mean step length \(\bar{\ell}\). perform_trials calls perform_single_walk n_trials times, accumulating \(D/\bar{\ell}\) for each, and returns the mean scaled distance.

random_driver.py

Orchestrates the full suite of experiments. Computes the number of experiments from max_steps, allocates result arrays, and fills them by calling perform_trials at each step count in the halving sequence.

run_random_walk_experiments( max_steps, n_trials) → (lengths, avgDist)

lengths[j] holds the step count and avgDist[j] holds the corresponding mean scaled distance. Arrays are returned in ascending order of step count (smallest steps first).

random_plot.py

Receives the two result arrays and renders a log-log scatter/line plot using matplotlib. Both axes are logarithmic; grid lines on both major and minor ticks help the eye judge the slope.

plot_random_walk_results( lengths, avgDist) → None (displays figure)

The plot title, axis labels, and grid are all set inside this module. No physics or driver logic appears here.

main.py

The user-facing entry point. Sets the two editable parameters, calls the driver, and passes the results to the plot module. All adjustments to program behavior begin here.

maxSteps = 4096 nTrials = 2000 lengths, avgDist = \ run_random_walk_experiments( maxSteps, nTrials) plot_random_walk_results( lengths, avgDist)
Naming convention: The Random program uses the module names random_physics.py, random_driver.py, and random_plot.py (prefix-first style), rather than the suffix-first style (physics_random.py, etc.) used in some other GFtGU programs. This avoids a name conflict with Python's built-in random module.

Parameters

Both parameters are set at the top of main.py. Edit them there before running the program.

Parameter Default Units Description
maxSteps 4096 steps (dimensionless) Maximum walk length. The program runs experiments at maxSteps, maxSteps/2, maxSteps/4, … down to 2. Must be a power of 2 for cleanest results; any integer ≥2 is accepted. Controls the upper end of the log-log plot and the total number of experiments (\(n_{\mathrm{walks}} = \lfloor \log_2(\texttt{maxSteps}) \rfloor\)).
nTrials 2000 trials (dimensionless) Number of independent random walks averaged at each step count. Larger values reduce statistical scatter and smooth the log-log curve; smaller values run faster but produce noisier results. Typical useful range: 100 – 10,000.
Run time: With the defaults the program executes roughly \(12 \times 2000 \times \overline{n_{\mathrm{steps}}}\) random-number draws. The most expensive experiment uses 4,096 steps × 2,000 trials ≈ 8.2 million draws; the full suite completes in a few seconds on a modern laptop.

Program Output

The program produces a single matplotlib figure: a log-log plot of average scaled distance \(\langle d \rangle\) (vertical axis) versus number of steps \(N\) (horizontal axis). Both axes carry the label "(log scale)" to remind the reader of the logarithmic scaling.

Data points are shown as filled circles connected by a line. The grid is drawn on both major and minor tick marks so that the slope of the data can be estimated visually. If the simulation is working correctly, the points should lie close to a straight line with slope \(\tfrac{1}{2}\), confirming the theoretical diffusion scaling law \(\langle d \rangle \propto \sqrt{N}\).

Reading the slope: On a log-log plot, a slope of \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) means that each time \(N\) increases by a factor of 4, \(\langle d \rangle\) doubles. Check this by comparing any two points separated by two halvings (i.e., a factor of 4 in step count): the mean distance should be approximately twice as large at the longer walk.

With maxSteps = 4096 and nTrials = 2000, the typical output shows 12 data points spanning step counts from 2 to 4096, with scaled distances ranging from roughly 1.1 (2 steps) to about 50 (4096 steps). Statistical fluctuations are small at 2000 trials, producing a smooth curve that closely follows the theoretical slope.

Suggested Experiments

Verify the \(\sqrt{N}\) Law

Run with defaults and inspect the log-log slope. Overlay a reference line with slope \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) (edit random_plot.py to add ax.plot(x, c*np.sqrt(x)) for a suitable constant \(c\)). The data should lie on top of the reference.

maxSteps = 4096 nTrials = 2000

Effect of Trial Count on Noise

Reduce nTrials dramatically and observe how much scatter appears in the log-log curve. Re-run several times with the same parameters to see run-to-run variability. At what trial count does the slope become reliably measurable?

maxSteps = 4096 nTrials = 50 # then 200, 2000

Extend the Step-Count Range

Double or quadruple maxSteps to add more data points at the long-walk end of the plot. Does the \(\sqrt{N}\) scaling hold over a wider range? Note the increase in run time for the longest experiments.

maxSteps = 16384 # 2^14 nTrials = 1000

1D and 2D Walks

Edit random_physics.py to perform a 1D walk (set \(\Delta y = \Delta z = 0\), net displacement = \(|X|\)) or a 2D walk (\(\Delta z = 0\)). The \(\sqrt{N}\) law is dimension-independent — do your modified results confirm this?

maxSteps = 4096 nTrials = 2000 # modify random_physics.py

Gaussian Steps

Replace the uniform draw 2*random.random()-1 with a Gaussian draw random.gauss(0, 1) in each component. The diffusion law is universal — does the slope remain \(\tfrac{1}{2}\)?

maxSteps = 4096 nTrials = 2000 # modify random_physics.py

Solar Photon Estimate

Use the result to estimate how many steps a photon takes to traverse the Sun. The solar radius is about \(7 \times 10^8\) m; the mean free path in the solar interior is roughly 1 cm = \(10^{-2}\) m. If \(\langle d \rangle = R_\odot / \lambda_{\mathrm{mfp}}\), what is \(N\)? What travel time does that imply?

maxSteps = 4096 nTrials = 2000 # N ~ (R/lambda)^2
