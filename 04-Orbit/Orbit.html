Description

Orbit computes the trajectory of a test particle (planet, satellite, or comet) moving in the gravitational field of a fixed central mass under Newton's law of gravity. The central mass is treated as stationary and located at the origin of the coordinate system; the test particle's position and velocity are integrated forward in time using a predictor-corrector scheme with an adaptive time-step.

The default configuration reproduces Mercury's orbit around the Sun, starting from perihelion at \(r = 4.6 \times 10^{10}\) m with the perihelion speed of 58 980 m s−1. With equal-axis scaling the output is a closed ellipse—a direct numerical demonstration of Kepler's first law.

Five output modes let the user examine the orbit shape, velocity hodograph, position or velocity as functions of time, and kinetic/potential/total energy as functions of time. Orbit is the foundation program for all later orbital investigations in the book; Binary, RelativisticOrbit, and the three-body programs are all straightforward extensions of the same core integration logic.

Physical Background

Newton's Law of Gravitation

A body of mass \(M\) located at the origin exerts a gravitational acceleration on a test particle at position \(\mathbf{r} = (x, y)\) directed radially inward with magnitude \(GM/r^2\), where \(G = 6.674\times10^{-11}\) N m² kg⁻² is Newton's gravitational constant and \(r = |\mathbf{r}|\). The program uses the composite parameter \(k = GM\) so that the acceleration components become:

Newton's gravitational acceleration
\[ a_x = -\frac{k\,x}{r^3}, \qquad a_y = -\frac{k\,y}{r^3}, \qquad r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} \]

For the default (Sun), \(k = GM_\odot = 1.327\times10^{20}\) m³ s⁻². Changing k to \(GM_\oplus = 3.986\times10^{14}\) m³ s⁻² models Earth-centered orbits (the Moon, satellites); any central body can be substituted.

Bound and Unbound Orbits

The character of the orbit is determined entirely by the specific mechanical energy (energy per unit mass):

Specific mechanical energy
\[ \mathcal{E} = \frac{1}{2}v^2 - \frac{k}{r} \]
  • \(\mathcal{E} < 0\) — bound orbit (ellipse); the particle returns periodically.
  • \(\mathcal{E} = 0\) — parabolic escape; marginal case, cometary in character.
  • \(\mathcal{E} > 0\) — hyperbolic flyby; the particle escapes to infinity.

A remarkable feature of \(1/r^2\) gravity (Bertrand's theorem) is that all bound orbits are closed ellipses—this is not true for any other power law. The program verifies closure by tracking the angular position through each orbit.

Kepler's Laws (Numerical Verification)

By running the program for different planets (using the perihelion distances and speeds in Table 4.3 of Schutz) the user can verify:

  • First law — orbits are closed ellipses (equal-axis plot).
  • Second law — equal areas swept in equal times (visible from position-vs-time output).
  • Third law — \(T^2 \propto a^3\), period increases with semi-major axis.

Governing Equations

Equations of Motion

The state vector is \((x,\, y,\, v_x,\, v_y)\). Newton's second law gives four coupled first-order ODEs:

Equations of motion
\[ \dot{x} = v_x, \qquad \dot{y} = v_y \] \[ \dot{v}_x = a_x = -\frac{k\,x}{r^3}, \qquad \dot{v}_y = a_y = -\frac{k\,y}{r^3} \]

Predictor Step (Constant-Acceleration Approximation)

At the start of each timestep \(\Delta t\), accelerations \((a_{x0}, a_{y0})\) are computed at the current position. A first-order predictor gives a trial endpoint:

Predictor — trial position
\[ \Delta v = a_{x0}\,\Delta t, \quad \Delta u = a_{y0}\,\Delta t \] \[ \delta x = v_x\,\Delta t + \tfrac{1}{2}\Delta v\,\Delta t, \quad \delta y = v_y\,\Delta t + \tfrac{1}{2}\Delta u\,\Delta t \] \[ x_1 = x + \delta x, \quad y_1 = y + \delta y \]

Adaptive Timestep Control (eps1)

Accelerations \((a_{x1}, a_{y1})\) at the trial endpoint are compared with those at the start. If the fractional change (sum-of-absolute-values norm) exceeds the threshold eps1, the step is rejected and \(\Delta t\) is halved:

Timestep rejection test
\[ |a_{x1} - a_{x0}| + |a_{y1} - a_{y0}| \;>\; \varepsilon_1 \bigl(|a_{x0}| + |a_{y0}|\bigr) \]

Halving repeats until the test passes. The timestep is never increased automatically; it is only shortened when accuracy demands it.

Predictor-Corrector Iteration (eps2)

Once the timestep is accepted, the corrector iterates (up to 10 times) using the average of the beginning and predicted endpoint accelerations:

Corrector — improved velocity increment
\[ \Delta v = \tfrac{1}{2}(a_{x0} + a_{x1})\,\Delta t, \quad \Delta u = \tfrac{1}{2}(a_{y0} + a_{y1})\,\Delta t \] \[ \delta\delta x_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}\Delta v\,\Delta t, \quad \delta\delta y_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}\Delta u\,\Delta t \]

The iteration converges when the change in the corrector increment satisfies:

Corrector convergence test
\[ |\delta\delta x_1 - \delta\delta x_0| + |\delta\delta y_1 - \delta\delta y_0| \;\leq\; \varepsilon_2 \bigl(|\delta\delta x_0| + |\delta\delta y_0|\bigr) \]

Committed Step

After convergence the state is advanced using the corrected increments:

State update
\[ x \leftarrow x + v_x\,\Delta t + \delta\delta x_1, \quad y \leftarrow y + v_y\,\Delta t + \delta\delta y_1 \] \[ v_x \leftarrow v_x + \Delta v, \quad v_y \leftarrow v_y + \Delta u \]

Specific Energy (Energy Output Mode)

Because the test-particle mass cancels, the program reports energy per unit mass:

Specific kinetic, potential, and total energy
\[ \mathcal{K} = \tfrac{1}{2}v^2, \qquad \mathcal{U} = -\frac{k}{r}, \qquad \mathcal{E} = \mathcal{K} + \mathcal{U} \]

\(\mathcal{E}\) should remain constant throughout the integration; deviations indicate numerical error.

Algorithm

Overview

The integration proceeds step-by-step in simulated time. For each step the driver:

  1. Computes accelerations at the current position (physics module call).
  2. Runs the predictor to obtain a trial new position.
  3. Tests the adaptive timestep criterion; if it fails, halves \(\Delta t\) and retries from step 1.
  4. Runs the predictor-corrector loop (≤ 10 iterations) until convergence.
  5. Commits the position and velocity update.
  6. Runs the orbit-closure test; stops if one full orbit has been completed or maxSteps reached.

Orbit-Closure Detection

Closure detection uses the polar angle \(\theta = \mathrm{atan2}(y, x)\). After the first step the algorithm tracks the sign of \(\theta\):

  • A flag halfOrbit is set once \(\theta\) goes negative (the orbit has passed through the lower half-plane).
  • A flag fullOrbit is set once \(\theta\) returns positive after halfOrbit is true — i.e., the orbit has come back around.
  • When fullOrbit fires, the orbit counter nOrbits increments; the simulation stops when nOrbits ≥ 1.

This two-flag scheme handles both clockwise and counter-clockwise motion and avoids false closure at the very first step.

Tip: The program stops naturally after one complete orbit of a bound trajectory. For unbound (hyperbolic) orbits it runs until maxSteps is exhausted. You can simulate multiple orbits by increasing maxSteps and leaving the orbit counter inactive, or by running successive calls from main.py.

Accuracy Notes

The eps1 and eps2 parameters control independent aspects of accuracy:

  • eps1 = 0.05 (default) is relatively loose. Tightening to 0.005 or 0.001 will increase step count but give a more faithful ellipse.
  • eps2 = 1×10⁻⁴ is the corrector convergence tolerance; it rarely needs tuning for standard planetary orbits.
  • Setting either parameter to a large value (e.g. 10) disables the corresponding correction entirely — useful for understanding what each contributes.

Module Overview

Physics Layer
physics_orbit.py

Encodes Newton's gravitational acceleration law. The only literal number is the exponent in the inverse-square formula; the gravitational parameter \(k = GM\) is always passed in as an argument so the physics is independent of any specific body.

def compute_acceleration(x, y, k) -> (ax, ay)
Driver Layer
driver_orbit.py

Implements the full predictor-corrector integration loop with adaptive timestep and orbit-closure detection. Returns structured NumPy arrays appropriate for the selected output mode.

def run_orbit( xInit=4.6e10, yInit=0.0, vxInit=0.0, vyInit=58980.0, k=1.327e20, dt0=1e4, maxSteps=20000, eps1=0.05, eps2=1e-4, output="orbit" ) -> arrays (mode-dependent)
Plot Layer
plot_orbit.py

Receives the structured arrays from the driver and produces matplotlib figures. Dispatches on the output mode string to select the appropriate plot type (orbit trace, hodograph, time series, or energy curves).

def plot_orbit(result, output="orbit") -> matplotlib figure (displayed)
Entry Point
main.py

User-editable entry point. Set the initial conditions, gravitational parameter, timestep, accuracy controls, and output mode here, then run this file to execute the simulation and display the plot.

from driver_orbit import run_orbit from plot_orbit import plot_orbit result = run_orbit(...) plot_orbit(result, output=...)

Parameters

All parameters are set in main.py as arguments to run_orbit(). Defaults reproduce Mercury's orbit around the Sun.

Parameter Default Units Description
xInit 4.6e10 m Initial x-position (Mercury perihelion distance from Sun)
yInit 0.0 m Initial y-position; start on the x-axis for a canonical perihelion launch
vxInit 0.0 m s⁻¹ Initial x-velocity; zero for a perpendicular perihelion launch
vyInit 58980.0 m s⁻¹ Initial y-velocity; Mercury's maximum orbital speed at perihelion
k 1.327e20 m³ s⁻² Gravitational parameter \(GM\) for the central body (default: Sun)
dt0 1e4 s Initial timestep; reduced automatically by eps1 control if needed
maxSteps 20000 — Maximum integration steps; limits runtime for unbound orbits
eps1 0.05 — Fractional-change threshold for adaptive timestep halving (sum-of-abs norm)
eps2 1e-4 — Convergence threshold for predictor-corrector iteration
output "orbit" — Output mode; see Output Modes section for all choices
Common substitutions: Earth-centered orbits: set k = 3.986e14 (m³ s⁻²). For the Moon at perigee: xInit=3.626e8, vyInit=1082. For a comet near-parabolic orbit: set speed close to the escape speed \(v_\text{esc} = \sqrt{2k/r}\).

Output Modes

The output parameter passed to run_orbit() selects what arrays are returned and how plot_orbit() renders them.

Mode string Returns Plot produced
"orbit" xs, ys Trajectory in the x-y orbital plane; displays as an ellipse for bound orbits
"velocity" vx, vy Velocity hodograph — the curve traced by \((v_x, v_y)\) through the orbit; always a circle for Newtonian gravity
"position_time" ts, xs, ys x-position and y-position vs. time; period readable from the repeating pattern
"velocity_time" ts, vx, vy x-velocity and y-velocity vs. time; confirms the orbit is periodic in velocity too
"energy" ts, KE, PE, TE Specific kinetic (\(\mathcal{K}\)), potential (\(\mathcal{U}\)), and total (\(\mathcal{E}\)) energy vs. time; total should be constant — deviations reveal numerical error
Verification tip: Run with output="energy" and examine how flat the total energy curve is. Tighten eps1 and note the improvement; this is a direct measure of numerical energy conservation.

Suggested Experiments

EXP-1

Reproduce the Solar System

Use the perihelion distances and speeds from Table 4.3 of Schutz to set xInit and vyInit for each planet. Confirm that each output orbit is a closed ellipse and compare the computed eccentricity to the tabulated value.

EXP-2

Measure Orbital Periods

Switch to output="position_time". Read the period from the x-position time series. Verify Kepler's third law \(T^2 \propto a^3\) across multiple planets.

EXP-3

Velocity Hodograph

Set output="velocity" and plot the velocity-space curve. For a Newtonian \(1/r^2\) force, the hodograph is a perfect circle—one of the elegant consequences of the inverse-square law discovered by Hamilton.

EXP-4

Bound vs. Unbound Orbits

Starting from Mercury's perihelion, gradually increase vyInit. At the escape speed \(v_\text{esc} = \sqrt{2k/\texttt{xInit}}\approx 75\,800\) m s⁻¹ the orbit opens into a parabola; above it, a hyperbola.

EXP-5

Direction Independence of Binding

Keep the speed fixed but rotate the initial velocity vector (adjust vxInit and vyInit so that \(v_x^2 + v_y^2\) is constant). Verify that the orbit changes shape but bound/unbound character does not — a consequence of energy conservation.

EXP-6

Energy Conservation Accuracy

Run with output="energy" and progressively tighten eps1 from 0.5 to 0.001. Plot how the total-energy drift scales with eps1; this demonstrates that accuracy improves systematically with the control parameter.

EXP-7

Disable Corrections

Set eps1 = 10 (disable adaptive timestep) and eps2 = 10 (disable predictor-corrector). Observe how the orbit drifts and energy fails to conserve — a vivid illustration of what the two accuracy techniques accomplish.

EXP-8

Moon Around Earth

Set k = 3.986e14, xInit = 3.626e8 (perigee distance), vyInit = 1082 m s⁻¹, and scale dt0 to ~100 s. The output should be a nearly circular orbit with a period of ≈ 27.3 days.

Related Programs

  • Binary — Extends Orbit to the full two-body problem where both bodies orbit their common centre of mass. Uses the same predictor-corrector integration but treats two mutually attracting bodies.
  • RelativisticOrbit — Adds the leading-order general-relativistic (Schwarzschild) correction term to the Newtonian acceleration. Produces the famous perihelion precession absent from this Newtonian version.
  • SphereGravity — Uses the same gravitational acceleration law to verify the shell theorem: a uniform spherical shell exerts no net force on a particle inside it and acts like a point mass on a particle outside.
  • Multiple_RK45 — An N-body integrator using a Runge-Kutta-Fehlberg RK45 adaptive step solver. Extends the two-body idea to arbitrary numbers of mutually interacting masses.
