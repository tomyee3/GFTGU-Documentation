What This Program Does

SphereGravity computes the gravitational acceleration produced by a thin spherical shell of matter at a series of radial distances ranging from deep inside the shell to far outside it. The calculation is purely numerical: the shell is divided into ndiv × ndiv small tiles, each tile is treated as a point mass, and their individual gravitational contributions are summed to give the total acceleration at each observation radius.

The program can display either the raw acceleration profile or the relative difference between the numerical result and the exact Newtonian prediction. The latter mode makes it easy to see how rapidly the computation converges to the analytical answer as the number of tiles is increased.

Physical Background

One of Newton's most beautiful results in the Principia is the shell theorem: a uniform spherical shell of mass \(M\) and radius \(R\) exerts exactly the same gravitational force on any external test mass as if all of \(M\) were concentrated at the shell's center. Furthermore, a test mass placed anywhere inside the hollow shell experiences no net gravitational force at all.

These results have profound consequences throughout physics and astronomy. They allow us to treat the Earth, Sun, and any other spherically symmetric body as point masses when computing orbits. They also underlie the equivalence principle arguments used in cosmology, where matter enclosed within a sphere of radius \(r\) is the only matter relevant for dynamics at that radius.

Newton proved the theorem analytically. Here we verify it numerically: because any spherically symmetric body can be decomposed into a nested set of thin shells, proving the result for a single thin shell suffices for the general case, as Schutz explains in Investigation 4.3.

Newton's Shell Theorem

For a thin spherical shell of total mass \(M\) and radius \(R\), the gravitational acceleration \(g\) at radial distance \(r\) from the center is:

Eq. 1 — Shell theorem (exact Newtonian result)
\[ g(r) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{GM}{r^2} & r > R \quad (\text{outside the shell}) \\[10pt] 0 & r < R \quad (\text{inside the hollow shell}) \end{cases} \]

The program uses dimensionless units throughout: the shell radius is set to \(R = 1\), the shell's surface density \(\rho = 1\), and \(G = 1\). The shell's relative thickness (ratio of physical thickness to radius) is \(\varepsilon = 0.001\), small enough that the thin-shell approximation is excellent.

Governing Equations

Shell geometry and tile masses

The shell is parameterised by latitude angle \(\theta\) (measured from the equator, ranging from \(-\pi/2\) at the south pole to \(+\pi/2\) at the north pole) and azimuthal angle \(\varphi\). The shell is divided into \(n_\text{div}\) equal steps in each coordinate. The angular step sizes are:

Eq. 2 — Angular step sizes
\[ \Delta\varphi = \frac{2\pi}{n_\text{div}}, \qquad \Delta\theta = \frac{\Delta\varphi}{2} \]

Each tile at latitude \(\theta\) has a surface area element \(\Delta A = \cos\theta\, \Delta\theta\,\Delta\varphi\) (in units where \(R = 1\)). The mass of each tile is:

Eq. 3 — Tile mass
\[ \delta m = \varepsilon\,\rho\,R^2\,\cos\theta\;\Delta\theta\;\Delta\varphi \;\longrightarrow\; \varepsilon\,\cos\theta\;\Delta\theta\;\Delta\varphi \quad (R = \rho = 1) \]

All \(n_\text{div}\) tiles in a ring of constant latitude are identical, so the ring's contribution to the total mass is \(n_\text{div}\,\delta m\). The total shell mass, computed by summing all rings, evaluates analytically to \(M = 4\pi\varepsilon\).

Distance from tile to observer

By the azimuthal symmetry of the problem the observation point is placed at position \(r\) along the shell's symmetry axis. The distance from a tile at latitude \(\theta\) to the observer at radius \(r\) is:

Eq. 4 — Distance from tile to observer
\[ d(\theta,r) = \sqrt{1 + r^2 + 2r\sin\theta} \]

Axial component of acceleration

The gravitational acceleration toward the center of the shell contributed by a single ring of tiles is:

Eq. 5 — Ring contribution to radial acceleration
\[ \delta g = n_\text{div}\;\frac{G\,\delta m\;(r + \sin\theta)}{d(\theta,r)^3} \]

where the factor \((r + \sin\theta)\) is the projection of the tile-to-observer vector onto the symmetry axis. The total acceleration at radius \(r\) is:

Eq. 6 — Total numerical acceleration
\[ g_\text{num}(r) = \sum_{k=0}^{n_\text{div}-1} \delta g_k \]

Relative difference

When the relative difference output mode is selected, the program reports:

Eq. 7 — Relative difference from Newton's theorem
\[ \delta(r) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{g_\text{num}(r) - g_\text{Newton}(r)}{g_\text{Newton}(r)} & r > 1 \quad\bigl(g_\text{Newton} = M/r^2\bigr)\\[10pt] \dfrac{g_\text{num}(r)}{M} & r \leq 1 \quad \text{(inside: Newton predicts zero)} \end{cases} \]

Outside the shell the Newtonian acceleration is \(g_\text{Newton} = M/r^2\) (with \(G=1\)). Inside the shell Newton predicts exactly zero, so the raw numerical acceleration is normalised by \(M\) (the Newtonian acceleration just outside the surface) to produce a dimensionless measure of the residual error.

Tiling Algorithm

The computation follows a straightforward nested-loop scheme described in Investigation 4.3 of Gravity from the Ground Up:

  1. Initialise geometry. Compute the angular step sizes \(\Delta\varphi\) and \(\Delta\theta\), and set the initial latitude \(\theta_0 = \tfrac{1}{2}\Delta\theta - 90°\) (centre of the first tile, just above the south pole).
  2. Compute total shell mass. Loop over all \(n_\text{div}\) latitude rings. For each ring accumulate \(\Delta M_k = n_\text{div}\;\delta m_k\). This gives the normalisation constant \(M\) used in relative-difference mode.
  3. Outer loop: radial positions. Step through 1 000 observation radii \(r_j = 0.1\,j\) for \(j = 0, 1, \ldots, 999\) (the range \(r \in [0, 99.9]\), with the shell at \(r = 1\)).
  4. Skip the shell surface. The step at \(j = 10\) (i.e., \(r = 1.0\)) falls exactly on the shell. Newtonian gravity is not well-defined there (it depends on shell thickness and the exact location within the shell), so this point is skipped and its acceleration is set to zero.
  5. Inner loop: tile summation. For each \(r_j\), loop over all \(n_\text{div}\) latitude rings. Compute \(d_k = \sqrt{1 + r_j^2 + 2\,r_j\,\sin\theta_k}\) and add the ring's contribution (Eq. 5) to the running sum \(g_\text{num}(r_j)\).
  6. Post-process for output mode. If relative difference is selected, convert each acceleration value using Eq. 7. Otherwise output the raw acceleration array.
Computational cost: The inner loop executes \(n_\text{div}\) times for each of the 1 000 radii, so the total work scales as \(\mathcal{O}(1000 \times n_\text{div})\). The mass-summation loop adds another \(\mathcal{O}(n_\text{div})\). For \(n_\text{div} = 10\,000\) this is \(10^7\) floating-point operations — fast on modern hardware but noticeable. Note that this is different from Schutz's Java description, which quotes \(n_\text{div}^2\) tiles; here each latitude ring contributes \(n_\text{div}\) tiles but they are accounted for analytically (all tiles in a ring are identical), so the actual loop count is \(n_\text{div}\), not \(n_\text{div}^2\).

Accuracy and Convergence

The key parameter controlling accuracy is nDiv. As it increases the tiles become smaller and their point-mass approximation improves. Schutz's Java program shows the following convergence behaviour in the relative-difference output, which the Python port reproduces:

nDiv Max relative error (just outside shell) Run-time scaling
100≈ 3 × 10−2baseline
1 000≈ 10−510×
10 000≈ 10−7100×

Because the error arises from approximating smooth integrals by a finite Riemann sum, the convergence rate is approximately \(\delta \sim n_\text{div}^{-2}\): doubling the number of divisions roughly quarters the error.

Module Overview

The program is split into four Python modules following the standard GFtGU separation of concerns:

Physics

physics_spheregravity.py

Encodes the shell-tiling algorithm and the gravitational summation. All physical formulas (Eqs. 2–7) live here. The only literal numbers in this module are physical constants: \(\pi\) and the conversion factor \(\pi/180\).

compute_shell_mass(nDiv, epsilon=0.001) → float
compute_acceleration_profile(nDiv, outputType='acceleration', epsilon=0.001) → (list, list)
Driver

driver_spheregravity.py

Thin orchestration layer that calls the physics module and returns the (radius, acceleration) arrays. Provides a clean interface so that main.py and the plot module are decoupled from the physics internals.

run_spheregravity(nDiv=100, outputType='acceleration') → (list, list)
Plot

plot_spheregravity.py

Accepts the (radius, acceleration) arrays from the driver and renders a matplotlib figure. Axis labels and title adapt automatically to the selected output mode.

plot_spheregravity(radius, acceleration, outputType='acceleration') → None
Entry Point

main.py

The only file the user needs to edit. Set nDiv and outputType, then run python main.py. Calls run_spheregravity() and passes the result to plot_spheregravity().

nDiv = 500
outputType = 'relative difference'

Parameters

All user-adjustable parameters are set in main.py. The physics module also exposes epsilon as an optional keyword argument; it is fixed at 0.001 for normal use.

Parameter Default Type Description
nDiv 500 int Number of angular divisions per coordinate. The shell is divided into ndiv latitude rings, each containing ndiv tiles. Larger values give higher accuracy at the cost of longer computation time. Slider maximum in Schutz's original GUI was 10 000.
outputType 'relative difference' str Selects what the plot displays. Either 'acceleration' (raw \(g(r)\)) or 'relative difference' (fractional error from Newton's theorem per Eq. 7).
epsilon 0.001 float Fractional thickness of the shell (\(\varepsilon = \text{thickness}/R\)). Does not affect the accuracy of the point-mass approximation, but does scale the total mass \(M = 4\pi\varepsilon\). Fixed in the original Java; exposed as a keyword argument in the Python port.

Output Modes

acceleration

Plots the computed gravitational acceleration \(g_\text{num}(r)\) versus radius \(r\). Outside the shell (\(r > 1\)) you will see the characteristic \(1/r^2\) falloff; inside (\(r < 1\)) the acceleration is essentially zero. The shell radius \(R = 1\) is the boundary between the two regions.

relative difference

Plots the fractional deviation between the numerical result and Newton's exact prediction (Eq. 7). This mode is more informative for convergence studies: as nDiv increases the curve should drop closer and closer to zero everywhere. Inside the shell, where Newton predicts \(g = 0\), the residual acceleration normalised by \(M\) is plotted instead.

Quick Start

Running the program

cd SphereGravity
python main.py

With the default settings (nDiv = 500, outputType = 'relative difference') the program computes the acceleration profile over 1 000 radii and opens a matplotlib window. The plot shows the fractional error in the shell-theorem computation, which should be on the order of \(10^{-6}\) at its worst.

Switching output mode

# In main.py — show raw acceleration instead of relative difference
nDiv       = 100
outputType = 'acceleration'

Running programmatically

from driver_spheregravity import run_spheregravity
from plot_spheregravity   import plot_spheregravity

radius, accel = run_spheregravity(nDiv=1000, outputType='relative difference')
plot_spheregravity(radius, accel, outputType='relative difference')

Suggested Experiments

The following scenarios are drawn from Schutz's suggestions in Chapter 4 and extended with additional numerical investigations:

1 · Verify the Shell Theorem Visually

Run with the default parameters and outputType = 'acceleration'. Confirm that the acceleration is zero for \(r < 1\) and follows a \(1/r^2\) curve for \(r > 1\). The discontinuity at \(r = 1\) (the shell surface, skipped by the program) should be apparent.

nDiv=500 · outputType='acceleration'

2 · Convergence Study: Powers of Ten

Switch to outputType = 'relative difference' and run with nDiv = 10, 100, 1000, 10000 in succession. Observe the maximum relative error decrease from roughly 30% down to \(\sim 10^{-7}\), confirming \(\delta \propto n_\text{div}^{-2}\).

nDiv=10 / 100 / 1000 / 10000 · outputType='relative difference'

3 · Worst-Case Error Location

At low accuracy (small nDiv) the relative error peaks just outside the shell at \(r \approx 1.1\). Verify this by plotting with nDiv = 100 and locating the maximum of \(|\delta(r)|\). As nDiv grows the peak migrates and shrinks.

nDiv=100 · outputType='relative difference'

4 · Inside the Shell: Residual Acceleration

With outputType = 'relative difference', examine the values for \(r < 1\). These should be very small (ideally zero), but numerical discretisation gives a non-zero residual. Watch how this residual shrinks as nDiv increases, providing a second convergence indicator.

nDiv=100 / 1000 · focus on r < 1

5 · Effect of Shell Thickness

Vary the optional epsilon parameter in physics_spheregravity.py (e.g., 0.1, 0.01, 0.001). Note that epsilon scales the total mass \(M\) but should not affect the relative difference output, confirming that the shell-theorem result is independent of thickness.

epsilon=0.1 / 0.01 / 0.001 · outputType='relative difference'

6 · Ellipsoidal Shell (Advanced)

Schutz's suggested modification: replace the spherical shell with an axially-symmetric ellipsoidal shell. The shell-theorem result (zero interior acceleration) still holds for ellipsoids, but the exterior field differs from a point mass. Modify physics_spheregravity.py to parameterise the shell by an axis ratio \(a/b\) and verify the interior result.

custom: replace r=1 shell with r=sqrt(cos²θ + (a/b)²sin²θ)
