Overview

Binary simulates the orbital motion of two gravitationally bound point masses under Newtonian gravity. Given the initial positions and velocities of both bodies, the program integrates the coupled equations of motion forward in time using a predictor–corrector algorithm with an adaptive timestep.

The simulation records the position, velocity, and total mechanical energy of each body at every accepted step, and produces seven distinct plot types — from orbital trajectories and velocity-space diagrams to time-series of position, velocity, and energy. Integration stops when the orbit closes (the bodies return to within a threshold of their initial separation) or when the maximum step count is reached.

Scope: Binary is a purely Newtonian simulation. No relativistic corrections are applied. For post-Newtonian or Schwarzschild-metric counterparts see the MercPert and RelativisticOrbit programs in this collection.

Quick Start

Requirements

Python 3.9 or later with matplotlib:

pip install matplotlib

Running the Program

Clone or download the repository, then:

cd GFTGU-Programs/Binary
python main.py

The default configuration uses two equal-mass bodies, \(M_A = M_B = 2\times10^{30}\,\mathrm{kg}\) (approximately one solar mass each), placed symmetrically at \(x = \pm\,1\times10^{11}\,\mathrm{m}\) with equal and opposite transverse velocities of \(\pm\,1.5\times10^{4}\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\). This produces a nearly circular mutual orbit.

Choosing Output

Edit the output_type variable near the bottom of main.py to select which plot is displayed. See Output Types for the complete list of accepted string values.

Program Files

main.py
Entry Point & Configuration

Defines every initial condition and simulation parameter — masses, starting positions, starting velocities, the maximum timestep dt, the step limit max_steps, and the two tolerance thresholds eps1 and eps2. After calling integrate_binary() to run the simulation it passes the result to plot_binary() for display. All user-adjustable settings live here; no other file needs editing for typical use.

physics_binary.py
Physics Engine

Defines the BinaryState dataclass, which stores the current time together with the positions \((x_A, y_A,\, x_B, y_B)\) and velocities \((v_A, u_A,\, v_B, u_B)\) of both bodies. Provides three functions: relative_displacement() returns the separation vector \((\Delta x,\,\Delta y)\) and scalar distance \(r\); accelerations() evaluates the Newtonian gravitational acceleration on each body; and energies() computes the instantaneous kinetic, gravitational potential, and total mechanical energy of the system.

driver_binary.py
Integration Driver

Defines the BinaryResult dataclass (time-series lists of positions, velocities, and energies for both bodies) and the function integrate_binary(). The driver advances the system step by step using the Euler predictor followed by the averaged-acceleration corrector, with fractional-change convergence testing and adaptive timestep control. It monitors the inter-body separation at every step and terminates early when orbit closure is detected. All numerical method logic lives in this module.

plot_binary.py
Visualisation

Provides the plot_binary() function and the OutputType string literal type. Accepts a BinaryResult and renders one of seven plots via matplotlib: orbital trajectories in the \(xy\)-plane (equal aspect ratio), velocity-space diagrams, position vs. time, or velocity vs. time for each body individually, and the full energy budget vs. time. All plots carry axis labels, titles, and legends.

Two-Body Problem

Two point masses \(M_A\) and \(M_B\) interact solely through Newtonian gravitational attraction. Newton's law of universal gravitation gives the magnitude of the mutual force:

(1) \[ F \;=\; \frac{G\,M_A\,M_B}{r^2} \]

where \(G = 6.6726\times10^{-11}\,\mathrm{N\,m^2\,kg^{-2}}\) is Newton's gravitational constant and

(2) \[ r \;=\; \sqrt{(x_A - x_B)^2 + (y_A - y_B)^2} \]

is the scalar separation between the two bodies. The motion is confined to the \(xy\)-plane. Following the convention used in the source code, \(v\) denotes the \(x\)-component of velocity and \(u\) denotes the \(y\)-component.

Coordinate convention: The code stores each body's state as \((x,\,y,\,v,\,u)\) — that is, \(v = \dot x\) and \(u = \dot y\) — following the original Schutz Java convention.

With equal masses and symmetric initial conditions the centre of mass remains at the origin throughout the simulation. Varying the mass ratio or the initial transverse speed produces elliptical orbits of arbitrary eccentricity.

Equations of Motion

Applying Newton's second law component by component, with the force directed along the line joining the two bodies, yields four coupled second-order ODEs. Define the displacement of A relative to B: \(\Delta x = x_A - x_B\), \(\Delta y = y_A - y_B\).

Body A

(3) \[ \ddot x_A \;=\; -\frac{G\,M_B\,\Delta x}{r^3}, \qquad \ddot y_A \;=\; -\frac{G\,M_B\,\Delta y}{r^3} \]

Body B

(4) \[ \ddot x_B \;=\; +\frac{G\,M_A\,\Delta x}{r^3}, \qquad \ddot y_B \;=\; +\frac{G\,M_A\,\Delta y}{r^3} \]

The sign flip between bodies reflects Newton's third law: the force on B is equal and opposite to the force on A. These accelerations are computed by accelerations() in physics_binary.py and evaluated at every stage of the predictor–corrector loop.

The complete system state at any instant is the eight-component vector \((x_A,\,y_A,\,v_A,\,u_A,\,x_B,\,y_B,\,v_B,\,u_B)\), stored in the BinaryState dataclass and advanced one accepted timestep at a time by integrate_binary().

Energy Conservation

The total mechanical energy is the sum of the kinetic energies of both bodies and their mutual gravitational potential energy.

Kinetic Energy

(5) \[ K \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,M_A\!\left(v_A^2 + u_A^2\right) + \tfrac{1}{2}\,M_B\!\left(v_B^2 + u_B^2\right) \]

Gravitational Potential Energy

(6) \[ U \;=\; -\frac{G\,M_A\,M_B}{r} \]

Total Mechanical Energy

(7) \[ E \;=\; K + U \]

For a gravitationally bound orbit \(E < 0\) and is a conserved quantity. Any systematic drift in \(E\) over the run is a direct measure of accumulated numerical error. The energy vs. time plot is therefore the primary diagnostic for assessing integration quality.

Tip: If the total energy shows a noticeable drift, try reducing dt, tightening eps1, or tightening eps2 in main.py. Each of these increases the number of steps or corrector iterations per orbit and typically reduces energy drift by roughly one order of magnitude per factor-of-ten tightening.

Orbit Closure

The driver monitors the inter-body separation \(r\) at every accepted step. Once a sufficient fraction of the first orbit has elapsed the driver tests whether the bodies have returned close to their starting configuration. Let \(r_0\) be the initial separation. The closure condition is:

(8) \[ \bigl|\, r - r_0 \,\bigr| \;<\; \delta\, r_0 \]

When this is satisfied, integration terminates early, capturing essentially one complete orbit without unnecessary additional steps. If no closure is detected before max_steps steps have been taken, integration stops at that limit instead.

Eccentric orbits: For highly elliptical orbits the adaptive timestep contracts sharply near periapsis, where the gravitational acceleration is large and rapidly varying, and expands near apoapsis. This concentrates computational effort where it is most needed and keeps the energy error small throughout the full orbit.

Predictor–Corrector

The integration scheme is a two-stage Adams-style predictor–corrector. Let superscript \(n\) denote values at the beginning of the current step and \(n+1\) the values to be determined. The working timestep is \(\Delta t\).

Stage 1 — Euler Predictor

An explicit Euler step provides a first estimate of the velocities and positions at \(t^{n+1}\). For body A:

(9) \[ \tilde v_A = v_A^{\,n} + a_{x,A}^{\,n}\,\Delta t, \qquad \tilde u_A = u_A^{\,n} + a_{y,A}^{\,n}\,\Delta t \]
(10) \[ \tilde x_A = x_A^{\,n} + v_A^{\,n}\,\Delta t, \qquad \tilde y_A = y_A^{\,n} + u_A^{\,n}\,\Delta t \]

and equivalently for body B. The gravitational accelerations \(\tilde a_{x,A},\,\tilde a_{y,A},\,\tilde a_{x,B},\,\tilde a_{y,B}\) are then evaluated at the predicted positions \((\tilde x_A,\,\tilde y_A,\,\tilde x_B,\,\tilde y_B)\).

Stage 2 — Averaged-Acceleration Corrector

The corrector refines the velocity estimates by averaging the accelerations at the start-of-step and predicted states. For body A:

(11) \[ v_A^{\,n+1} \;=\; v_A^{\,n} + \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(a_{x,A}^{\,n} + \tilde a_{x,A}\right)\Delta t \]
(12) \[ u_A^{\,n+1} \;=\; u_A^{\,n} + \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(a_{y,A}^{\,n} + \tilde a_{y,A}\right)\Delta t \]

and equivalently for body B.

Position Update

Positions are updated using the trapezoidal (averaged-velocity) rule:

(13) \[ x_A^{\,n+1} \;=\; x_A^{\,n} + \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(v_A^{\,n} + v_A^{\,n+1}\right)\Delta t \]
(14) \[ y_A^{\,n+1} \;=\; y_A^{\,n} + \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(u_A^{\,n} + u_A^{\,n+1}\right)\Delta t \]

and equivalently for body B.

After the position update, the accelerations are re-evaluated at the corrected positions and the corrector loop repeats. The loop runs for at most 10 iterations per step, exiting early once the fractional change in all four acceleration components falls below \(\varepsilon_2\) (see Convergence Criteria).

Adaptive Timestep

Once the corrector loop has converged, the driver assesses step quality by computing the maximum fractional change in the gravitational accelerations between the initial Euler predictor estimate and the final corrected state. Denoting this quantity \(\Delta_a\):

(15) \[ \Delta_a \;=\; \max\!\left( \frac{\bigl|\tilde a_{x,A} - a_{x,A}^{\,n+1}\bigr|} {\bigl|a_{x,A}^{\,n+1}\bigr|},\; \frac{\bigl|\tilde a_{y,A} - a_{y,A}^{\,n+1}\bigr|} {\bigl|a_{y,A}^{\,n+1}\bigr|},\; \frac{\bigl|\tilde a_{x,B} - a_{x,B}^{\,n+1}\bigr|} {\bigl|a_{x,B}^{\,n+1}\bigr|},\; \frac{\bigl|\tilde a_{y,B} - a_{y,B}^{\,n+1}\bigr|} {\bigl|a_{y,B}^{\,n+1}\bigr|} \right) \]

Step Rejection

If \(\Delta_a > \varepsilon_1\), the step is rejected. The working timestep is halved and the entire predictor–corrector cycle is retried from the unchanged current state:

(16) \[ \Delta t \;\leftarrow\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\Delta t \qquad \bigl(\Delta_a > \varepsilon_1\bigr) \]

Step Growth

If the step is accepted (\(\Delta_a \le \varepsilon_1\)), the working timestep grows by 10% toward the user-specified ceiling \(\Delta t_{\max}\):

(17) \[ \Delta t_{\mathrm{work}} \;\leftarrow\; \min\!\bigl(1.1\,\Delta t_{\mathrm{work}},\;\Delta t_{\max}\bigr) \qquad \bigl(\Delta_a \le \varepsilon_1\bigr) \]

This strategy automatically concentrates steps near periapsis, where accelerations change most rapidly, and relaxes them near apoapsis, keeping energy conservation uniform throughout each orbit at minimal computational cost.

Convergence Criteria

Two independent tolerance parameters control integration quality. Both are set in main.py.

Parameter Symbol Default Role
eps1 \(\varepsilon_1\) \(10^{-2}\) Adaptive timestep gate. Steps whose maximum fractional acceleration change \(\Delta_a\) exceeds this value are rejected and retried at half the timestep.
eps2 \(\varepsilon_2\) \(10^{-3}\) Corrector convergence test. The corrector loop exits when the fractional change in every acceleration component between successive iterations falls below this value (or after 10 iterations).
Design relationship: \(\varepsilon_2\) should be strictly smaller than \(\varepsilon_1\) so that the corrector fully converges before the timestep controller evaluates \(\Delta_a\). The defaults \(\varepsilon_1 = 10^{-2}\), \(\varepsilon_2 = 10^{-3}\) satisfy this. Tightening both tolerances by a factor of ten typically reduces energy drift by roughly two orders of magnitude at the cost of additional step halvings near periapsis.

Parameters

All parameters are defined near the top of main.py.

Variable Default value Unit Description
MA \(2\times10^{30}\) kg Mass of body A (≈ 1 M☉)
MB \(2\times10^{30}\) kg Mass of body B (≈ 1 M☉)
xInitA \(-1\times10^{11}\) m Initial \(x\)-position of body A
xInitB \(+1\times10^{11}\) m Initial \(x\)-position of body B
yInitA, yInitB \(0\) m Initial \(y\)-positions — both bodies start on the \(x\)-axis
vInitA, vInitB \(0\) m s−1 Initial \(x\)-velocities — no initial radial motion
uInitA \(+1.5\times10^{4}\) m s−1 Initial \(y\)-velocity of body A (transverse)
uInitB \(-1.5\times10^{4}\) m s−1 Initial \(y\)-velocity of body B (transverse, opposite)
dt \(1\times10^{4}\) s Maximum timestep; the adaptive scheme may use smaller values
max_steps \(5000\) — Maximum integration steps before forced termination
eps1 \(10^{-2}\) — Adaptive timestep tolerance \(\varepsilon_1\)
eps2 \(10^{-3}\) — Corrector convergence tolerance \(\varepsilon_2\)

Output Types

Assign one of the following string literals to output_type in main.py:

  • "orbits" Orbital trajectories of both bodies in the \(xy\)-plane. The axes are set to equal aspect ratio so that circular orbits appear circular.
  • "velocity space" Velocity-space trajectories — \(u\) vs. \(v\) for each body. For circular orbits these traces are circles; elliptical orbits produce ellipses.
  • "position vs. time body A" \(x_A(t)\) and \(y_A(t)\) as functions of time for body A.
  • "position vs. time body B" \(x_B(t)\) and \(y_B(t)\) as functions of time for body B.
  • "velocity vs. time body A" \(v_A(t)\) and \(u_A(t)\) as functions of time for body A.
  • "velocity vs. time body B" \(v_B(t)\) and \(u_B(t)\) as functions of time for body B.
  • "energy vs time" Kinetic \(K(t)\), gravitational potential \(U(t)\), and total mechanical energy \(E(t)\) vs. time. This is the primary diagnostic plot for assessing numerical accuracy.
