Overview

Neutron builds a complete model of a neutron star's internal structure by integrating the equations of general-relativistic hydrostatics outward from the stellar center to the surface. Unlike the orbital programs in this collection (Binary, Multiple, MercPert), Neutron does not evolve a system through time; instead it computes a static, spherically symmetric equilibrium configuration — the unique structure that results from a given central pressure and equation of state.

The central physics ingredient is the Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff (TOV) equation, which is the general-relativistic replacement for Newtonian hydrostatic equilibrium. It accounts for the fact that in a sufficiently compact object, pressure itself contributes to gravity — an effect that is negligible for ordinary stars but crucial for neutron stars. This feedback between pressure and gravity is responsible for the famous maximum mass of neutron stars: beyond a critical central pressure, adding more mass actually reduces the star's size until it collapses to a black hole.

The program uses a polytropic equation of state, \( p = K\rho^\gamma \), which captures a broad class of stellar models with two parameters: the polytropic exponent \(\gamma\) and the proportionality constant \(K\). By varying these parameters the user can explore normal neutron stars, strange-quark-matter stars, and ultra-stiff or ultra-soft nuclear equations of state.

How this program differs from the others in this collection Neutron is a radial structure solver, not a time-domain ODE integrator. There is no predictor–corrector loop, no RK45 adaptive stepper, and no orbit to complete. The independent variable is radius \(r\) (not time), and the integration marches outward from \(r = 0\) until pressure first becomes negative — that point is the stellar surface.

Parameters

All parameters are set near the top of main.py and passed to the compute_neutron_star() driver function. No command-line interface or GUI is provided; edit the values directly in the source file.

Parameter Default Units Description
gamma 5/3 dimensionless Polytropic exponent \(\gamma\) in \(p = K\rho^\gamma\). \(\gamma = 5/3\) corresponds to a non-relativistic, degenerate neutron gas. Use \(\gamma = 1\) with \(K = c^2/3\) to model strange-quark matter.
pC 1e34 Pa Central pressure \(p_C\) (pressure at \(r = 0\)). This is the primary free parameter determining the star's mass and radius. Larger \(p_C\) gives a more massive, more compact star.
K 1e-15 SI (Pa · m3γ kg−γ) Polytropic proportionality constant. Together with \(\gamma\) it determines how stiff the equation of state is. Larger \(K\) produces larger, less dense stars at the same central pressure.
output_type "Pressure" string Selects the plotted quantity. Must be exactly one of "Pressure", "Density", or "Mass".
Physical constants (hard-coded in physics_neutron.py)
  • \(G = 6.672 \times 10^{-11}\) N m2 kg−2 — Newton's gravitational constant
  • \(c^2 = 8.98755 \times 10^{16}\) m2 s−2 — speed of light squared
These match the values used in Schutz's original Java program and in the textbook examples.

Output Modes

The program always integrates the full stellar structure (pressure, density, and enclosed mass as functions of radius). The output_type parameter selects which of the three profiles is displayed in the matplotlib figure.

output_typey-axis quantityy-axis label
"Pressure" Pressure \(p(r)\) Pressure (Pa)
"Density" Mass density \(\rho(r)\) Density (kg/m³)
"Mass" Enclosed mass \(m(r)\) Mass interior (kg)

The x-axis is always the radial coordinate in metres. The figure title reads "Neutron Star: <output_type>". To see all three profiles, change output_type and re-run; no recomputation is needed because the driver already returns all three arrays.

Module Structure

The program is divided into four Python modules following the same architecture as the other programs in this collection:

main.py

Entry point. Sets all user-adjustable parameters and calls the driver and plot functions.

  • Imports compute_neutron_star from driver_neutron
  • Imports plot_neutron from plot_neutron
  • Sets gamma, pC, K, output_type
  • One-line call: data = compute_neutron_star(gamma, pC, K)
  • One-line plot: plot_neutron(data, output_type)

driver_neutron.py

Orchestrates the radial integration. Implements the marching loop, step-doubling logic, surface detection, and array trimming. Returns a dictionary of trimmed arrays ready for plotting.

  • compute_neutron_star(gamma, pC, K) → dict
  • Computes \(\rho_C\), scale height, and initial step size \(dr\)
  • Fills arrays: radius, pressure, density, mass
  • Stops integration when \(p < 0\) (surface reached)
  • Doubles dr and retries if surface not reached in 2000 steps
  • Returns dict with key last_step and trimmed arrays

physics_neutron.py

Defines the three physics kernels — the equation of state, the TOV pressure step, and the mass increment — plus the physical constants \(G\) and \(c^2\).

  • eos_density(p, K, gamma) — polytropic inversion: \(\rho = (p/K)^{1/\gamma}\)
  • tov_pressure_step(p, rho, m, r, dr) — one radial step of the TOV equation
  • mass_step(m, rho, r, dr) — one radial step of the mass integral
  • Constants: G = 6.672e-11, c2 = 8.98755e16

plot_neutron.py

Produces the matplotlib figure. Reads the data dictionary returned by the driver and plots the selected profile versus radius.

  • plot_neutron(data, output_type)
  • 8 × 6 inch figure, line width 2, grid on
  • x-axis: "Radius (m)"
  • y-axis label and title determined by output_type
  • Raises ValueError for unrecognised output_type

Newtonian Hydrostatics

An ordinary star is in hydrostatic equilibrium: at each radius the outward pressure gradient exactly balances the inward gravitational pull of the mass below. In Newtonian gravity this condition reads

\[ \frac{dp}{dr} = -\frac{G\,m(r)\,\rho}{r^2}, \tag{1} \] (1)

where \(m(r)\) is the mass enclosed within radius \(r\), \(\rho\) is the local mass density, and \(G\) is Newton's gravitational constant. Equation (1) is the foundation of the companion program Star in this collection.

For white dwarfs and most ordinary stars, equation (1) is adequate. But for neutron stars, where \(p\) approaches \(\rho c^2\) and the gravitational radius \(r_g = 2GM/c^2\) is comparable to the physical radius, two Newtonian approximations break down simultaneously:

  • Pressure contributes to the source of gravity through Einstein's equations — it is not merely a restoring force.
  • The geometry of space inside a compact object is curved; the Newtonian formula for enclosed volume underestimates the actual amount of matter.

These two effects are captured by the Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff equation.

The Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff (TOV) Equation

Derived from Einstein's field equations for a static, spherically symmetric perfect fluid, the TOV equation replaces Newtonian hydrostatics for relativistic stellar interiors:

\[ \frac{dp}{dr} = -\frac{G\!\left(\rho + \dfrac{p}{c^2}\right) \!\left(m + \dfrac{4\pi r^3 p}{c^2}\right)} {r\!\left(r - \dfrac{2Gm}{c^2}\right)}. \tag{2} \] (2)

Comparing with the Newtonian equation (1), three corrections are immediately visible:

  1. \(\rho \to \rho + p/c^2\). Pressure itself carries energy, and energy gravitates. At nuclear densities, \(p \sim \rho c^2\), so this correction can double the effective gravitational source.
  2. \(m \to m + 4\pi r^3 p/c^2\). The pressure inside the star contributes to the gravitational mass felt by a shell at radius \(r\). This "pressure mass" is negligible in Newtonian stars but significant in compact ones.
  3. Schwarzschild factor \((r - 2Gm/c^2)\) in the denominator. As the gravitational radius \(r_g = 2Gm/c^2\) approaches the physical radius \(r\), the pressure gradient diverges — the star can no longer be held up and collapses. This is the GR origin of the neutron-star mass limit.
Implementation note — Schutz's simplified form The denominator \(r(r - 2Gm/c^2)\) in equation (2) is algebraically equivalent to \(r^2(1 - 2Gm/rc^2)\), the standard form found in many GR texts. Schutz factors the \(r^2\) into the numerator to give the compact form used in the Java original, and the Python port faithfully reproduces this factored expression in tov_pressure_step().

The TOV equation is integrated numerically by tov_pressure_step() using a forward-Euler step of size \(dr\): 

def tov_pressure_step(p, rho, m, r, dr):
    numerator   = G * (rho + p / c2) * (m + 4.0 * math.pi * r**3 / c2)
    denominator = r * (r - 2.0 * G * m / c2)
    return p - numerator * dr / denominator

Each call advances the pressure by one radial step: \(p_{j} = p_{j-1} - (dp/dr)\,\Delta r\).

Polytropic Equation of State

To close the system of equations we need a relationship between pressure and density. The program uses a polytropic equation of state:

\[ p = K\,\rho^\gamma. \tag{3} \] (3)

Given the pressure \(p\) at each step, the density is recovered by inverting equation (3):

\[ \rho = \left(\frac{p}{K}\right)^{1/\gamma}. \tag{4} \] (4)

This inversion is implemented in eos_density(p, K, gamma) as (p / K) ** (1.0 / gamma).

Choice of \(\gamma\) and physical interpretation
  • \(\gamma = 5/3\) (default) — non-relativistic degenerate neutron gas. Appropriate for the outer layers of a neutron star.
  • \(\gamma = 2\) — a stiff, "hard" EOS; gives heavier, larger stars. Explores the effect of strong nuclear repulsion at high density.
  • \(\gamma = 1\), \(K = c^2/3\) — a gas of massless fermions (strange-quark matter / "MIT bag model"). Explores the hypothesis that the true ground state of cold matter is quark matter rather than nuclear matter.

Mass Integral

The mass enclosed within radius \(r\) satisfies the continuity equation, which in the weak-field Newtonian limit takes the familiar form:

\[ \frac{dm}{dr} = 4\pi r^2 \rho. \tag{5} \] (5)

(In full GR the right-hand side carries a metric factor, but for the purpose of this program Schutz uses the flat-space approximation for the mass increment, consistent with the level of the textbook.) One radial step gives:

\[ m_j = m_{j-1} + 4\pi r_{j-1}^2\,\rho_{j-1}\,\Delta r, \tag{6} \] (6)

implemented in mass_step(m, rho, r, dr) as m + 4.0 * math.pi * r**2 * rho * dr. The value data["mass"][-1] (the last element after trimming) gives the total gravitational mass of the star, in kg.

Buchdahl's Theorem

A purely GR result, due to Hans Buchdahl (1959), states that for any static, spherically symmetric fluid star in GR, the stellar radius \(R\) must satisfy:

\[ R \;\geq\; \frac{9}{4}\,\frac{GM}{c^2} \;=\; \frac{9}{8}\,r_g, \tag{7} \] (7)

where \(r_g = 2GM/c^2\) is the Schwarzschild (gravitational) radius. In other words, the physical radius of a stable star can be no smaller than \(\tfrac{9}{8}\) of its gravitational radius; equivalently, the gravitational radius cannot exceed \(\tfrac{8}{9}\) of the physical radius.

This bound is independent of the equation of state — it is a consequence of geometry and the energy conditions alone. For the default parameters in this program, you can verify Buchdahl's theorem by reading off the stellar radius (where pressure first vanishes) and comparing it with \((9/4)\,GM/c^2\) computed from the final enclosed mass.

The Maximum Mass of Neutron Stars

One of the most striking predictions of the TOV equation is that there is an absolute maximum mass for a cold neutron star in hydrostatic equilibrium. As the central pressure \(p_C\) is increased, the stellar mass \(M\) first rises, then reaches a maximum, and then decreases — beyond the peak, adding central pressure makes the star less massive and less stable. The critical configuration at the mass peak is unstable against radial perturbations and will collapse to a black hole.

For the default polytrope (\(\gamma = 5/3\), \(K = 10^{-15}\)) this maximum can be found by running a sequence of models with increasing \(p_C\) and plotting total mass versus central pressure. For realistic nuclear equations of state the maximum is typically in the range \(1.5\)–\(3\,M_\odot\), depending on the stiffness.

Historical note Oppenheimer and Volkoff (1939) derived the first quantitative maximum mass for a gas of free neutrons, obtaining \(M_\text{max} \approx 0.7\,M_\odot\). Modern equations of state that include nuclear forces push the limit to \(2\)–\(3\,M_\odot\). Observed pulsars with masses \(\sim 2\,M_\odot\) already rule out the softest proposed equations of state.

Radial Marching Scheme

Neutron star structure is determined by a system of two first-order ODEs in \(r\): the TOV pressure equation (2) and the mass continuity equation (5), coupled through the equation of state (3)–(4). The driver solves these by a simple forward-Euler radial march: starting at the stellar center and stepping outward by a fixed increment \(\Delta r\) until the surface is located.

At each radius step \(j\):

  1. Advance the pressure: \(p_j \leftarrow \texttt{tov\_pressure\_step}(p_{j-1}, \rho_{j-1}, m_{j-1}, r_{j-1}, dr)\)
  2. Check for surface: if \(p_j < 0\), record lastStep = j and break.
  3. Advance the enclosed mass: \(m_j \leftarrow \texttt{mass\_step}(m_{j-1}, \rho_{j-1}, r_{j-1}, dr)\)
  4. Invert the EOS: \(\rho_j \leftarrow \texttt{eos\_density}(p_j, K, \gamma)\)

The arrays are declared with a maximum of NMAX = 2000 steps and trimmed to [:lastStep] before being returned to the caller.

Forward Euler accuracy Forward Euler is first-order accurate and accumulates truncation error proportional to \(\Delta r\). The step size is chosen to be small enough that this error is acceptable for the illustrations in the textbook, but for high-precision stellar models a higher-order integrator (e.g. Runge–Kutta) would be preferable. Halving dr manually (by setting dr = scale / 800 in driver_neutron.py) is a quick way to check convergence.

Step Sizing and the Scale Height

The integration step size \(\Delta r\) is set automatically from a characteristic scale height of the star. At the center, the pressure gradient is dominated by the central density \(\rho_C\) and the central pressure \(p_C\). A natural length scale is obtained by balancing these:

\[ \ell = \frac{\sqrt{p_C / G}}{\rho_C}, \tag{8} \] (8)

where the central density is derived from the central pressure via the equation of state: \(\rho_C = (p_C / K)^{1/\gamma}\). The step size is then:

\[ \Delta r = \frac{\ell}{400}. \tag{9} \] (9)

With 400 steps per scale height and a maximum of 2000 steps, the integration covers up to \(5\,\ell\) before doubling is triggered — sufficient for most stellar models whose surface pressure falls to zero within a few scale heights of the center.

Initial Conditions at the Stellar Center

The TOV equation (2) is singular at \(r = 0\): the denominator vanishes. To avoid this singularity the driver does not attempt to start the integration at \(r = 0\); instead it sets the first grid point at \(r_1 = \Delta r\) and uses the analytically known small-\(r\) behaviour to initialise the arrays:

\[ r_1 = \Delta r, \quad p_1 = p_C, \quad \rho_1 = \rho_C, \quad m_1 = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi\,(\Delta r)^3\,\rho_C. \tag{10} \] (10)

The mass initialisation uses the fact that near the center a sphere of radius \(\Delta r\) filled uniformly at density \(\rho_C\) has mass \(\frac{4}{3}\pi(\Delta r)^3\rho_C\). This is a first-order approximation that becomes exact as \(\Delta r \to 0\).

Automatic Step Doubling

If the stellar surface (the radius at which pressure first becomes negative) is not reached within 2000 steps, the step size is doubled and the integration is restarted from scratch: 

# Conceptual outline of the while loop in driver_neutron.py
while lastStep == 0:
    dr = initial_dr
    # ... fill arrays ...
    if surface not reached in NMAX steps:
        dr *= 2           # double and retry

This doubling continues until the surface is found, at the cost of reduced radial resolution. It is not an adaptive accuracy control (as in RK45 or similar schemes) — it simply ensures the integration range is wide enough to encompass the whole star. If you find that your chosen parameters require many doublings, the resulting coarse grid may not resolve the stellar structure well, and you should consider whether the parameters describe a physically reasonable star.

When does step doubling occur? Step doubling is triggered when the star is much larger than the estimated scale height \(\ell\). This can happen when \(K\) is very large (very stiff EOS), \(\gamma\) is very close to 1 (nearly incompressible), or \(p_C\) is unusually low. The default parameters (\(\gamma = 5/3\), \(p_C = 10^{34}\) Pa, \(K = 10^{-15}\)) do not require doubling.

Suggested Experiments

1. Mass–Central-Pressure Sequence (Finding the Maximum Mass)

Run the program repeatedly with output_type = "Mass", increasing pC from \(10^{33}\) to \(10^{36}\) Pa in steps of a factor of 3. For each run, note the value of data["mass"][-1] (total mass in kg; divide by \(1.989 \times 10^{30}\) to convert to solar masses). Plot total mass vs.\ \(p_C\) and identify the maximum. Beyond the peak, the TOV equation predicts an unstable branch.

2. Strange Quark Stars

Set \(\gamma = 1\) and \(K = c^2/3 \approx 2.996 \times 10^{16}\) m2 s−2. This models strange-quark matter, for which the EOS is \(p = (\rho c^2 - \rho_0 c^2)/3\), approximated here by a linear polytrope. Quark stars are predicted to have significantly different mass–radius relations from neutron stars — compare the surface radii and total masses with the \(\gamma = 5/3\) case.

3. Comparison with Newtonian Hydrostatics (Star Program)

The companion program Star (Chapter 4 of GFtGU) integrates the Newtonian hydrostatic equation (1) with the same polytropic EOS. Running both programs with identical \(\gamma\), \(K\), and \(p_C\) reveals the importance of the GR corrections: the Newtonian version overestimates the radius and underestimates the central density for the same central pressure. At the highest pressures the Newtonian star does not collapse — it just keeps growing — while the TOV star reaches an instability.

4. Verifying Buchdahl's Theorem

After running any model, compute the gravitational radius \(r_g = 2GM_\text{tot}/c^2\) using the total mass from data["mass"][-1], and compare it with the stellar radius (the last element of data["radius"]). Buchdahl's theorem requires \(R \geq (9/4)(GM/c^2) = (9/8)\,r_g\). For the default parameters this ratio should comfortably exceed 9/8; try to push \(p_C\) until the ratio approaches the Buchdahl bound.

5. Effect of EOS Stiffness

Vary \(\gamma\) between 1.2 and 3 while keeping \(K\) and \(p_C\) fixed. A larger \(\gamma\) represents a stiffer EOS (pressure rises more steeply with density). Stiffer equations of state support heavier and larger stars and tend to give higher maximum masses. Plot the stellar radius and total mass as functions of \(\gamma\).

6. Minimum Neutron Star Mass

Decrease \(p_C\) until the total mass approaches a minimum. Very low central pressures correspond to small, low-mass neutron stars whose surfaces are reached after very few steps. The minimum mass for a stable neutron star (set by the weak-force neutron-decay threshold) is of order \(0.1\,M_\odot\); explore how close your polytropic model gets.
